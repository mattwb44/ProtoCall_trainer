House fire at 03:05. Fire is rolling out of the first-floor living room windows on
side Alpha. A police officer on scene says a woman outside is screaming that her
father sleeps in the back corner bedroom upstairs. The interior stairs rise directly
over the living room. You are the outside team of the first-due truck; the engine is
stretching its line now.

## Stage: The Case for the Window

1. [common] (text) Why is this fire the textbook case FOR vent-enter-isolate-search — and
   what specific fact would make you choose the interior stairs instead?
   > Model answer: Known victim, known room, and an interior path that crosses directly
   > over the fire — the stairs are the flow path and may already be untenable, while
   > the target window puts you feet from the victim without touching the fire's
   > territory. You'd take the stairs instead if the line were already knocking the fire
   > and the stairwell were clean — VEIS is the answer when the interior route is worse
   > than the window, not a default for every bedroom.

2. [Firefighter] (text) You're heading to the rear corner with a ladder. What are you
   reading on the way — what would make this window a no-go?
   > Model answer: The window itself: fire or high-velocity dark smoke pushing from it
   > means the room is already part of the fire and entry is a fatality, not a rescue.
   > Also the room's position over the fire (floor integrity), and what's between this
   > room and the fire. Turbulent hot smoke at the top of the glass: marginal — take the
   > glass, read what lifts, decide at the sill. Calm smoke or clear glass: go.

3. [Officer] (text) Coordinate this VEIS with the engine's attack in one radio
   transmission — what must the interior crew know before your glass breaks?
   > Model answer: "Truck outside team performing VEIS, floor 2 side Charlie rear
   > bedroom, taking the window in thirty seconds." The engine must know because your
   > window becomes a new exhaust opening the moment it breaks — if their attack or door
   > timing depends on controlled ventilation, your entry just changed the fire's math.
   > Uncoordinated VEIS is freelancing with a ladder.

## Stage: Vent, Enter, Isolate

4. [Firefighter] (text) Glass is taken, sash cleared to the frame. Describe the sequence
   from sill to search — and say which single step defines VEIS versus ordinary window
   entry.
   > Model answer: Sound the floor from the ladder, sweep below the sill for a victim
   > (they collapse at exits), enter low, and go FIRST to the room's door and close it —
   > that isolation step is the definition of the tactic. With the door open you're
   > searching in a flow path that your own window is accelerating; with it closed the
   > room is a protected box that buys you and the victim minutes. Then search off the
   > door, back toward your window.

5. [common] (text) The bedroom door won't fully close — it's blocked by fallen clothing
   and swollen in the frame, leaving a hand-width gap. Smoke is starting to pull through
   the gap toward your window. What is your read, and your move?
   > Model answer: The gap is now an intake feeding your exhaust — the flow path is
   > forming through this room, and conditions will worsen on a timer. Clear the
   > blockage with one hard effort; if it still won't seat, jam it as closed as it goes
   > (the gap still throttles the flow substantially) and cut the search plan to the
   > high-probability spots — bed, beside the bed, path to the door — fast. Report the
   > degraded isolation so command knows your clock shortened.

6. [Firefighter] (text) You find the man unresponsive between the bed and the wall — a
   190-pound dead lift, and your partner is at the window. How does the removal actually
   work?
   > Model answer: Drag, don't lift: webbing or wrists, along the floor to the window,
   > victim's head kept away from the gap-side of the room. At the sill it becomes a
   > two-person mechanical problem — you feed him out headfirst into your partner on the
   > ladder, partner controls the descent with the victim's weight on the beam. Call for
   > a second ladder or hands at the base BEFORE you're holding him at the sill, not
   > while.

## Stage: The Fire Weighs In

7. [common] (text) As the victim reaches the ground, the engine radios: "line burst, no
   water on the fire, re-stretching." The fire below is now free-burning again. You're
   still in the room. What just happened to your situation?
   > Model answer: Your margin evaporated: the fire owns the interior again, the floor
   > under this room is being attacked from below, and your compromised door is the only
   > thing between you and a re-energized flow path. This room is no longer searchable —
   > exit now, out your window, and transmit that the rest of floor 2's primary is NOT
   > done. The rescue succeeded; staying to finish the room converts success into a
   > second victim.

8. [Officer] (text) Command asks whether your team can VEIS the OTHER second-floor bedroom
   for a possible second occupant, given no water is currently on the fire. Make the
   go/no-go call with your reasoning.
   > Model answer: No-go as asked — VEIS with zero water on a free-burning fire below is
   > outside the tactic's assumptions; the first entry worked partly because a line was
   > about to control the fire. Conditional yes: the moment the re-stretched line is
   > flowing, the second window with a fresh read of its smoke. Offer command that
   > sequencing rather than a flat refusal — the answer is "not yet and here's the
   > trigger," which is what command actually needs.

9. [common] (text) Debrief: the crew argues VEIS "worked so well we should lead with it
   more often." Push back with the honest boundaries of the tactic.
   > Model answer: It worked because every precondition was met: known victim, known
   > room, readable window, coordinated line, and an interior path worse than the
   > ladder. VEIS is a targeted rescue tool, not a search strategy — it covers one room
   > per commitment, puts crews above fire on isolated toeholds, and each broken window
   > re-plumbs the fire's ventilation. Lead with it when the case matches this one;
   > default to it and one day the room behind the glass is the fire room.
