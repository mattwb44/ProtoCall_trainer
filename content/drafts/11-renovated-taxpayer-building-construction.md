Fire reported in a restaurant occupying half the ground floor of a two-story
brick-and-joist commercial building, built around 1925, 08:50 — before opening.
Smoke is showing from the restaurant's rear. The block face shows three different
storefront eras, a parapet, and second-floor windows that don't line up with the
first-floor walls.

## Stage: What the Building Says

1. [common] (text) Before anyone goes in: what does "1925 ordinary construction, renovated
   several times" predict about how this building will behave in a fire? List the specific
   hazards that construction history creates.
   > Model answer: Ordinary construction means brick walls carrying wood joists — fire
   > travels in the joist bays and vertical chases, and the walls stay up while the
   > inside falls out of them. A century of renovations adds: concealed voids between
   > every remodel layer (tin ceilings under drop ceilings), abandoned openings between
   > occupancies, illegal or undocumented structural changes, and load paths nobody
   > alive understands. You're not fighting the building on the plans; you're fighting
   > every building it has ever been.

2. [Officer] (text) The second-floor windows not aligning with first-floor walls caught
   your eye on arrival. Why does that detail matter enough to change your orders?
   > Model answer: Misaligned openings suggest interior walls were moved — which in a
   > joist building means load-bearing elements may have been cut, headered off, or
   > removed entirely during a renovation, possibly without permits. Floors above the
   > restaurant may be carried by improvised framing. Orders change: nobody works under
   > the second floor without knowing what's holding it up, and the TIC and an early
   > look above the fire are now reconnaissance priorities, not luxuries.

3. [Firefighter] (text) You're opening the restaurant's drop ceiling ahead of the line and
   find a second, older tin ceiling above it with fire glowing through its seams. What
   have you found, and what do you tell your officer?
   > Model answer: A renovation void — the fire is running the joist bay space between
   > ceiling generations, above the tin, which resists your hook and hides the fire's
   > true extent. Report exactly that: "fire in the void above a tin ceiling, extending
   > [direction], can't open it fast." This changes the fight from a room fire to a
   > structure fire; the officer needs to know the fire is in the building's bones, not
   > its contents.

## Stage: The Fire Uses the Building

4. [common] (text) Fire is confirmed running the ceiling void toward the adjoining shop.
   Where else is it going that nobody can see, and what three places must be opened or
   checked right now?
   > Model answer: Vertically up every chase — plumbing walls, the old dumbwaiter or
   > stair shafts renovations love to hide — and laterally through the cockloft under
   > the roof, which in a taxpayer often runs the full block face. Check now: (1) the
   > second floor directly above the restaurant, (2) the cockloft via the roof scuttle
   > or top-floor ceiling, (3) the adjoining occupancy's ceiling void. In ordinary
   > construction the fire's address and its location are different facts.

5. [Officer] (text) The roof team reports the roof feels solid but they found three layers
   of roofing and a rubber membrane. Recalculate: what does that change about vertical
   ventilation and about collapse?
   > Model answer: Three layers plus membrane means a heavy roof on century-old joists —
   > added dead load the original structure never planned for — and a cut that takes
   > triple time while telling you little (the layers mask sponginess). Vertical vent is
   > now slower, costlier, and less reliable; weigh whether the cockloft can be hit from
   > below instead. For collapse: heavy roof + fire in the cockloft moves your timeline
   > up, and "feels solid" from above is exactly what failing built-up roofs report.

6. [Firefighter] (text) In the adjoining shop you find the shared wall has a doorway-sized
   hole covered by a bookcase — the occupants connected the two businesses informally.
   Why does this matter beyond fire spread?
   > Model answer: It voids the compartment logic everyone upstairs is depending on: the
   > "fire wall" between occupancies isn't one, so exposure assignments, search
   > assumptions, and the fire's predicted path are all built on a separation that has a
   > hole in it. It also warns that OTHER undocumented openings exist. Report it with
   > location; command must redraw the mental map of the building.

## Stage: The Collapse Decision

7. [common] (text) Twenty minutes in: fire has possessed the cockloft, the second floor is
   untenable, and streams are being repositioned. Describe the collapse sequence THIS
   building type produces, and what the early signs are.
   > Model answer: Ordinary construction typically fails inward-then-outward: joists burn
   > through and drop floors inside the shell, then unbraced brick walls — shoved by
   > collapsing floors or the parapet's own weight — fall into the street a wall-height
   > out. Early signs: smoke or water pushing through the brick's mortar joints, new
   > cracks, walls out of plumb, the parapet moving, and interior collapses you hear.
   > The parapet over the sidewalk is the sneakiest killer on this block face.

8. [Officer] (text) Command declares defensive and sets collapse zones. A shop owner is
   begging to retrieve a cash box from a storefront in the zone, and a news crew has set
   up inside it. Handle both, and say what "collapse zone" means numerically here.
   > Model answer: The zone is at least the wall's full height out from the wall —
   > add margin for the parapet and debris scatter. Nobody enters, and the news crew is
   > moved by police, not negotiation: brick walls give minutes of warning at most,
   > sometimes none. The owner gets a straight answer — nothing in the zone is worth a
   > body, and firefighters won't be sent for a cash box either. Enforcing the zone on
   > sympathetic people is the test of whether it's real.

9. [common] (text) Debrief question: this fire was lost in the first ten minutes, before
   any flame was visible outside. Argue that claim using only building-construction facts.
   > Model answer: The fire entered the joist voids and cockloft early — from that point
   > it occupied spaces hoselines couldn't reach without demolition-speed opening, in a
   > structure whose renovations meant every void connected to another. The heavy roof
   > made venting slow, the hidden wall breach voided compartmentation, and undocumented
   > structural changes made interior commitment above the fire indefensible. The
   > tactical lesson: in old ordinary construction, the fire you see is the receipt, not
   > the purchase — the building's construction decides the outcome unless you interrupt
   > the void fire early.
