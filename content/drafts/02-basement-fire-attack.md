Reported house fire at 18:10 on a weekday. You arrive to a two-story wood-frame
colonial with smoke showing from the first-floor windows and front door. A neighbor
says the family is usually home at this hour but no one is visible outside.

## Stage: Size-Up

1. [common] (text) Smoke shows on floor 1 but you see no fire. From the front, the smoke
   is uniform across the whole first floor. Before anyone stretches a line — what must
   you determine, and how?
   > Model answer: Where the fire actually is. Uniform smoke across an entire floor with
   > no visible fire is the classic signature of a fire below — in the basement — with
   > smoke pushing up through floor penetrations. A 360 is mandatory: look for basement
   > windows with smoke or fire, a walkout door, and heat signatures low. Stretching to
   > floor 1 and walking over a basement fire is how floors fail under crews.

2. [Officer] (text) Your 360 finds a walkout basement door at the rear with dark smoke
   pushing out the top of the frame. Give your initial radio report and your attack plan.
   > Model answer: Report a two-story wood-frame with a basement fire, walkout access at
   > the rear, offensive strategy, possible occupants. Plan: attack through the rear
   > walkout — hitting the fire at its level and from grade — while a second crew controls
   > the interior basement door on floor 1 to cut the flow path up the stairs. Announce
   > that no one operates on floor 1 over the fire area until the fire is knocked.

3. [Engineer] (text) Which line do you pull for this fire and why — and where's your
   second line going?
   > Model answer: A 1¾" with a smooth bore or straight stream is defensible for a
   > residential basement, but if the basement is loaded or well-involved, the 2½" buys
   > flow that overwhelms it fast from the door. The second line goes to floor 1 at the
   > interior basement stairs — protecting the flow path and the floor above the fire is
   > the backup line's whole job here.

## Stage: Attack

4. [common] (text) The rear basement door is opened for attack. What just happened to the
   fire from a flow-path perspective, and what must happen within seconds?
   > Model answer: You gave an underventilated fire a fresh intake, and the stairs to
   > floor 1 are now the exhaust — the fire will grow toward both openings. Water must go
   > in immediately behind the vent: flow from the doorway into the space, cool it, then
   > advance. Opening the door and pausing is the mistake; vent and attack are one action.

5. [Firefighter] (text) You're on the nozzle at the walkout. Heavy heat, zero visibility,
   fire glowing to your right past a wall of storage. How do you apply water before you
   advance?
   > Model answer: Flow from the door before committing — long bursts off the ceiling
   > into the glow, letting steam and the stream do work you can't do blind. Move in only
   > when the heat drops noticeably. Penciling in a basement with one way out is not
   > timidity; it's the difference between advancing behind cooled gases and crawling
   > into a flashover.

6. [Officer] (text) The floor-1 crew reports the floor feels "spongy" near the kitchen.
   The basement crew says they're making progress. What do you order?
   > Model answer: Floor-1 crew off that area now — spongy means the floor system is
   > burning or burned and sponginess is a late warning, not an early one. Have them hold
   > the stairwell door from a position on known-good flooring or exit entirely, and mark
   > the kitchen area as no-entry. The basement crew's progress doesn't restore burned
   > joists.

## Stage: Knockdown and Search

7. [common] (text) Fire is knocked. The family's whereabouts are still unknown. Where do
   you search first and why?
   > Model answer: Floor 1 and the path from the basement stairs — the flow path is where
   > smoke and heat went, and anyone home was breathing it. Then bedrooms on floor 2.
   > The basement itself gets searched by the attack crew as they overhaul. Search off
   > the flow path first: viability drops fastest where the exhaust ran.

8. [Firefighter] (text) During secondary search you find a locked bedroom door on floor 2,
   cool to the touch, no smoke behind it visible at the gap. How do you handle it?
   > Model answer: Force it and search it — locked and cool is exactly where a sheltering
   > occupant survives, and "no visible smoke" through a gap proves nothing. Control the
   > door behind you to keep the room clean, search fast and systematically, and report
   > the result either way so the search record is complete.

9. [Engineer] (text) Overhaul is starting and command asks you for a hydraulics check:
   the attack line has felt weak for ten minutes. Walk through your troubleshooting.
   > Model answer: Pump panel first — intake pressure and discharge pressure against what
   > the line needs; then walk the line for kinks (basement stretches kink at the door and
   > the stair turn); then check for a partially closed valve or debris at the nozzle.
   > Weak lines during overhaul get tolerated because the fire's out — that's exactly when
   > a rekindle finds an unprepared crew.
