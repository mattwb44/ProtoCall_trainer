You are first-due to a report of "smoke in the building" at a six-unit strip mall at
02:40. Businesses are closed. On arrival you see light gray smoke lazily pushing from
the door seams of three adjacent units.

## Stage: Arrival

1. [common] (text) Light gray smoke drifts from the door seams of units 3, 4, and 5 —
   none of them heavy, none of them fast. What is this smoke telling you about the fire's
   location and stage, and what do you announce?
   > Model answer: Smoke from multiple units with no clear point of origin at a strip
   > mall says the fire is likely above the ceiling in the common attic space,
   > not in any one occupancy. Lazy, light smoke means early-stage or oxygen-limited.
   > Announce a working fire with possible attic involvement so incoming companies
   > think roof and exposures, not a single-room job.

2. [Officer] (text) Do you commit crews to an interior investigation now, or hold at the
   door? State your reasoning in terms of what the smoke has and hasn't told you.
   > Model answer: Investigate with a charged line, but treat this as a trap until proven
   > otherwise: light smoke from multiple units means you don't know where the fire is,
   > and cockloft fires drop lightweight ceilings on crews. Pull ceiling immediately
   > inside the first unit to find it. Holding everyone outside wastes the early window;
   > committing deep without opening the ceiling is how crews get caught.

3. [Engineer] (text) No hydrant is visible on this side of the strip. What is your water
   supply plan for a building like this, and how much water should you assume you need?
   > Model answer: Assume commercial fire flow, not residential — a cockloft fire can own
   > all six units, so plan for 1,000+ GPM: secure a supply line before it's needed, call
   > the second-due to a second hydrant, and position so an aerial can still get the front.
   > Tank water is for the first line only, never the plan.

## Stage: Conditions Change

4. [common] (text) Ten minutes in, the smoke from unit 4 turns dark brown, thickens, and
   begins pulsing from the eaves in rhythm. What changed, and what does it mean for crews
   inside?
   > Model answer: Brown smoke is unfinished wood off-gassing — the structure itself is
   > now burning, and pulsing smoke means pressure swings from an underventilated fire
   > approaching flashover or backdraft conditions in the cockloft. Crews under that
   > ceiling are under a fuel-rich void ready to light. This is the "conditions changed —
   > reassess" moment: report it and expect the officer to pull crews back.

5. [Officer] (text) Given the change at the eaves, make the call: do interior crews stay
   or come out, and what do you transmit?
   > Model answer: Out. Transmit an emergency traffic conditions report — "all companies
   > exit unit 4, cockloft fire conditions deteriorating" — and account for every crew.
   > A pulsing, fuel-rich cockloft over a lightweight roof is a collapse-and-flashover
   > double threat; no property in these units is worth being under it. Reset to a
   > defensive-oriented plan and reassess from outside.

6. [Firefighter] (text) You're on the hook inside unit 3 when the order comes. You've
   just opened a ceiling bay and see glowing embers but no flame. What do you do on the
   way out?
   > Model answer: Close what you opened if you can do it in seconds — every bay you
   > leave open is a fresh air path feeding the cockloft — then exit with your crew and
   > report exactly what you saw and where. "Glow, no flame, bay above the register in
   > unit 3" is targeting data for the master streams.

## Stage: Defensive Reset

7. [common] (text) The strategy is now defensive. Where does the fire want to go, and
   what is the priority to cut it off?
   > Model answer: Laterally through the cockloft to the unburned units — cockloft fires
   > spread horizontally until they hit a fire wall or the end of the building. Priority
   > is a trench-cut-or-write-off decision: pick a defensible line (a real fire wall if
   > the building has one), open the roof ahead of the fire there if it's safe from
   > aerials, and flow water into the attic space on the fire side of that line.

8. [Engineer] (text) Command orders your master stream into unit 4. Where do you put the
   stream, and what do you watch for on the roofline?
   > Model answer: Through the front glass and off the underside of the roof deck so the
   > stream deflects into the cockloft — flowing into the showroom does nothing for a fire
   > above the ceiling. Watch the roofline for sag or a growing "smoke wave" along the
   > eaves: lightweight roofs fail early and quietly, and your stream position should
   > never depend on that roof staying up.

9. [Officer] (text) The owner of unit 6 arrives and begs you to save his shop. What can
   you honestly commit to, and how does it shape your tactics?
   > Model answer: Commit to fighting for the fire wall between the fire and his unit —
   > not to a guarantee. Tactically that means confirming where the nearest rated
   > separation actually is, getting ahead of the fire there, and protecting it with
   > opened ceiling and a hoseline on the unburned side if conditions allow crews at the
   > end unit. Honest, specific commitments keep trust when you can't save everything.
