title: Mayday on Division 2 — Running the Worst Radio Call
category: Fireground
subcategory: Residential
objective_primary: Command Presence
objective_secondary: Air Management

building_type: 2.5-story wood-frame single family, balloon frame

A working fire in a balloon-frame two-and-a-half story house, 22:40. Fire started in
the basement and has been running the stud channels. Attack is operating on floor 1,
a search crew is on floor 2, RIT is staged at side Alpha. Fifteen minutes in, a
partial floor collapse drops a searching firefighter from floor 2 into the floor-1
rear room. His partner is still on floor 2. You play this from all seats.

## Stage: The Call

1. [Firefighter] (text) You're the firefighter who fell: dropped one story, landing hard,
   your low-air alarm activating from the workout. You're conscious, pinned at the legs
   by flooring. Give the mayday transmission — the actual words — and then what you do
   in the sixty seconds after it.
   > Model answer: Key the mic and say the word three times, then the report — a LUNAR-
   > shaped version: "Mayday mayday mayday — [name/unit], floor 2 search, I've fallen
   > through to floor 1, rear room, pinned at the legs, air alarm activated, activating
   > my PASS." Then: manual PASS on, control your breathing DOWN (your air is now the
   > whole game), try self-extrication for real but stop if it spikes your breathing,
   > get your flashlight pointed up, and answer every radio call. The mayday is not
   > shameful and not premature — the ones that kill are the ones called two minutes
   > late.

2. [Officer] (text) You're command and the mayday just hit an operating fireground. Walk
   your first four transmissions/decisions in order — and name the classic command
   failure a mayday causes.
   > Model answer: (1) Acknowledge and take the report — get LUNAR out of him, keep him
   > talking; (2) deploy RIT to the last known location NOW; (3) move the fireground:
   > everyone else STAYS ON TASK — request the additional alarm, because the fire has
   > not paused, and either shift routine traffic to a second channel or clear the
   > channel per your system; (4) assign someone (a chief officer if available) as
   > rescue-group supervisor so you're not running two incidents on one brain. The
   > classic failure: the entire fireground converges on the mayday, freelancing, and
   > the fire — unfought — comes and takes the rescuers too.

3. [Firefighter] (text) You're the fallen firefighter's partner, still on floor 2 at the
   collapse edge, and you can hear him below you. Every instinct says jump down or dig.
   What is your actual job right now?
   > Model answer: Be the fix on his position — you are the only person on the
   > fireground who KNOWS where he is. Report it precisely ("partner directly below my
   > position, floor 2 rear bedroom, hole in the floor"), stay in voice contact with
   > him, guide RIT in by radio, and do NOT enter the hole: a second firefighter down
   > the same collapse converts a rescue into a recovery-times-two. Feed him
   > information and calm; feed RIT geometry. That discipline is the hardest and most
   > valuable act on this fireground.

## Stage: The Rescue Problem

4. [common] (text) RIT reaches him in four minutes. His air: 1,100 PSI and burning fast.
   Pinned legs will take time. What's the FIRST problem RIT solves, and why is it not
   the pin?
   > Model answer: Air. A pinned firefighter with a working supply has time; a freed
   > firefighter with an empty bottle has none — so the RIT pack goes on first:
   > connect the UAC or swap him onto the rescue cylinder, confirm flow, THEN start on
   > the entrapment. The sequencing rule for every firefighter rescue: secure the
   > survivable environment (air, then fire exposure), then extricate. Freeing a man
   > who suffocates during the lift is the failure mode this order exists to prevent.

5. [Officer] (text) You're the RIT officer at the hole. Size up the extrication: pinned
   legs under flooring, fire in the walls of a balloon-frame, one story to lift or one
   room to carry. Give your plan and your abort criteria.
   > Model answer: Plan: protect the room first — a hoseline dedicated to the rescue
   > area, because balloon-frame walls mean fire can arrive from below at any moment —
   > then brute-simple mechanics: crib and lift the flooring off the legs (airbags,
   > jacks, hands), package on his harness or a rescue sling, and remove HORIZONTALLY
   > through the rear room to the exterior door — never lift a story what you can drag
   > a room. Abort criteria stated up front: fire takes the room's walls or ceiling,
   > structural movement, or his air situation collapses — then it becomes a
   > grab-and-go regardless of the pin, with the lift trading finesse for speed.

6. [Engineer] (text) You're running the panel when the extra alarm hits and command
   re-tasks water: the attack line, the backup, and now a dedicated rescue line. What
   does the mayday change about your water and pressure management, and what's your
   quiet contribution to the rescue?
   > Model answer: The rescue line is now the priority discharge — it flows no matter
   > what else does, so recheck your supply margin (are you still hydrant-fed and
   > comfortable, or nearing capacity), set and verify its pressure, and resist the
   > urge to rob it when other lines call for more. Quiet contributions: perfect,
   > boring water; the rig's scene lighting pointed at the rescue access; and being
   > the calmest voice on the fireground when someone runs past the panel yelling.
   > Maydays are won by people doing their ordinary jobs extraordinarily steadily.

## Stage: After He's Out

7. [common] (text) He's out — conscious, leg-injured, breathing on the rescue bottle.
   What happens in the next ten minutes that command must NOT let slip, on both the
   medical and fireground sides?
   > Model answer: Medical: he goes to EMS as a trauma patient with full assessment and
   > transport — firefighters minimize in front of their crews, so the refusal option
   > basically doesn't exist here; his partner gets pulled and checked too (and watch
   > him — the guilt reaction is real). Fireground: a PAR — full personnel
   > accountability report — for every company, because collapses are rarely singular;
   > a structural reassessment of the building (offensive may be over); RIT restored
   > or replaced before interior work continues; and the fire, which has been winning
   > for ten distracted minutes, honestly re-evaluated. The mayday ending is not the
   > incident ending.

8. [Officer] (text) The formal debrief, a week later. As the incident commander, frame
   the three questions this mayday should make the department ask — about prevention,
   about the mayday itself, and about the rescue — and give the honest first-pass
   answers from this incident.
   > Model answer: Prevention: did we respect balloon-frame fire behavior — fire in the
   > stud channels means floors fail from below with little local warning; should
   > floor-2 operations have been restricted earlier or the channels opened sooner?
   > Probably yes. The mayday: was it called early and formatted well? Here, yes —
   > and that's worth saying loudly, because celebrating a well-called mayday is how
   > you get the next one called early. The rescue: did RIT sequence air-first,
   > protect-in-place, simple mechanics? Yes — drill the parts that were luck until
   > they're process. A department that only debriefs failures teaches its members to
   > hide the saves.
