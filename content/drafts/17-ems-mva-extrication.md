Two-vehicle T-bone at a suburban intersection, 17:50, rush hour. A sedan is caved in
on the driver's side with the driver trapped and moaning; the striking pickup's
driver is out and walking. Fluids on the road, traffic stacking, one bystander
already directing cars. You arrive on the first-due engine with extrication tools;
an ambulance and a second engine are behind you.

## Stage: Windshield Survey

1. [common] (text) Before anyone touches a car: what does your scene size-up cover at an
   MVA, and what's the specific kill-you hazard at this one?
   > Model answer: Traffic, fuel/fluids, fire potential, wires and poles, vehicle
   > stability, number of patients (look in, under, and AROUND both vehicles — ejected
   > and wandering patients get missed), and mechanism severity. The kill-you hazard
   > here is traffic: rush hour with a bystander playing traffic cop means the scene
   > isn't protected, and struck-by is the classic responder fatality at roadway
   > incidents. Blocking apparatus first, everything else second.

2. [Officer] (text) Give your arrival report and first three orders — protecting the
   scene, sizing the medical problem, and staging what's behind you.
   > Model answer: Report: two-vehicle T-bone, one trapped, extrication needed, request
   > police for traffic. Orders: engine parks as a blocker upstream at an angle, crew
   > stabilizes the sedan and pulls a precautionary line to guard the fluids; second
   > engine takes the far side or additional blocking; ambulance in the protected
   > pocket close to the sedan — the patient will come to them. Command at an MVA is
   > geometry first: build a protected workspace, then work.

3. [Firefighter] (text) You're assigned patient contact through the sedan's passenger
   side while extrication is set up. What's your job in there, and what can you do for a
   trapped patient before the door comes off?
   > Model answer: Be the inside medic: manual C-spine if indicated, airway watch,
   > primary survey as far as reach allows, bleeding control you can get to, cover them
   > with a blanket against glass and tool work, and TALK — narrate every loud thing
   > before it happens. You're also the crew's eyes on trend: "patient's answers are
   > slowing" from inside the car outranks any monitor outside it.

## Stage: The Extrication-Medicine Marriage

4. [common] (text) The driver: conscious, pale, belted, complaining of left chest and
   pelvis pain, pulse 118, pinned by the intruded door and dash. Translate that
   presentation into urgency — what's the medical clock, and what does it say to the
   extrication team?
   > Model answer: Lateral intrusion with chest and pelvis pain and tachycardia is
   > internal bleeding until proven otherwise — chest, pelvis, abdomen — and the pallor
   > plus 118 says the compensating has started. This is a load-and-go patient wrapped
   > in a car: the clock is minutes, and the extrication plan should be the FASTEST
   > acceptable path out, not the most elegant. Say it out loud to the tool operators:
   > "this patient is time-critical" changes cutting decisions.

5. [Engineer] (text) The extrication needs a plan B stated before tools work. Give the
   primary plan for a side-intrusion entrapment and the backup, and explain why glass
   and stabilization come first regardless.
   > Model answer: Primary: take the driver's door and B-post (side removal) for a
   > lateral extrication path. Backup: displace the dash if the lower entrapment
   > doesn't release, or take the roof for a vertical route. Stabilization and glass
   > management come first because every cut moves the car — an unstabilized vehicle
   > transmits tool forces into the patient's pinned pelvis, and uncontrolled glass
   > turns the patient compartment into a blender. Slow first, so fast works.

6. [Officer] (text) Three minutes into cutting, your inside firefighter reports the
   patient is now confused and her radial pulse is weak. The medic wants her out NOW;
   the extrication needs four more minutes done properly. Command decision — go.
   > Model answer: Compressing the timeline is now worth accepting risk: order the
   > rapid-extrication variant — the minimum additional cutting for ANY survivable
   > path, spinal ideals traded down to speed with manual stabilization, more hands on
   > the lift. Confusion plus a fading radial pulse is decompensated shock; four more
   > minutes of pretty extrication is a resource she doesn't have. Announce the change,
   > reassign hands, and tell the ambulance to be ready to roll on arrival at the door.

## Stage: Handoff and the Second Patient

7. [common] (text) She's out and the ambulance is loading. Deliver the handoff report to
   the transporting crew in under thirty seconds — what MUST be in it?
   > Model answer: Age/sex, mechanism (lateral intrusion, prolonged entrapment ~X
   > minutes), injuries found (left chest, pelvis), trend (alert → confused, radial
   > weakening — say the TREND, not just the last vitals), treatment given, and what's
   > NOT done yet. The trend line is the report's payload: a trauma team hearing
   > "deteriorating during extrication" makes different decisions than one hearing a
   > snapshot.

8. [common] (text) The pickup driver — the walking one — now says his neck is "a little
   stiff" and wants to just go home. Mechanism was significant. Close out this patient
   correctly, and name the trap the "obvious" patient creates.
   > Model answer: He gets a full assessment, documented — significant mechanism plus
   > new neck symptoms earns a real exam and a strong recommendation to be transported
   > or evaluated; refusal handled with capacity confirmed, risks explained in plain
   > words, and signed. The trap: the screaming trapped patient absorbs everyone, and
   > the quiet ambulatory one — who can be hiding a belt injury or a bleed behind
   > adrenaline — gets a wave-off. Walking is not an assessment finding; it's a
   > distraction.
