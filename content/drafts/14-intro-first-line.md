A fundamentals scenario: a daytime room-and-contents fire, everyone confirmed out,
done right step by step. Fire is showing from one bedroom window of a single-story
ranch at 13:40. You are on the first-due engine with a full crew. Untracked:
everyone answers everything.

## Stage: Setting Up the Stretch

1. [common] (text) One bedroom going in a ranch house. Which attack line comes off the
   engine, and what three factors drive line selection on any fire?
   > Model answer: The 1¾" preconnect — this is its exact design case. The three
   > factors: fire volume (one room needs roughly 150 GPM; the small line delivers it),
   > mobility (a ranch's hallways favor a line one or two firefighters can move fast),
   > and stretch distance (does the preconnect reach — check, don't assume). Line
   > selection is matching water to fire; the biggest line isn't the best line if it
   > arrives late or can't make the turn.

2. [common] (text) Where does the line enter — the front door, or the bedroom window
   where the fire is showing? Give the classic answer and its reasoning.
   > Model answer: The front door, classically: attacking from the unburned side puts
   > the line between the fire and the rest of the house (and any undiscovered
   > occupants), pushing the fight INTO the fire's territory instead of chasing it
   > deeper into the home. The window is the fire's exhaust — entering there puts you
   > behind the fire with the house as its next move. (A quick exterior hit through the
   > window before entry is a legitimate modern add-on — but the line still advances
   > through the door.)

3. [common] (text) The stretch: describe what "a good stretch" to the front door actually
   looks like, and name the two most common rookie stretch failures.
   > Model answer: The right amount of hose flaked clean at the door — enough working
   > line to reach the farthest room (estimate: to the door plus one length inside for
   > a ranch), laid in long loops with no kinks, nozzle and first coupling positioned
   > at the entry, crew masked before the door opens. The classic failures: short
   > stretching (line runs out in the hallway) and kinks at the door or corners —
   > a single hard kink can cut your flow by a third.

## Stage: Making the Attack

4. [common] (text) Before the nozzle firefighter opens the door: what does "check your
   nozzle" mean as a full pre-entry ritual?
   > Model answer: Bleed the line — crack the bail outside to blow the air out and
   > confirm water; check the pattern (straight stream or the fog setting your attack
   > calls for — and know which you have); confirm gauge pressure feels right in the
   > reaction; and glance back that your slack is with you. Discovering an airlocked
   > line or a fog pattern set to wide inside a burning hallway is a rookie rite of
   > passage nobody needs.

5. [common] (text) Advancing down the hall: heat banking down, glow at the bedroom
   doorway. Where exactly does your first water go, and why NOT directly onto the
   burning furniture first?
   > Model answer: Off the ceiling of the fire room — straight stream up at a steep
   > angle, sweeping — before hitting the contents. The ceiling holds the hottest gases
   > and the flashover threat; cooling overhead first kills the environment that can
   > kill you, and the deflecting stream rains onto the contents anyway. Then drop to
   > the seat and finish it. Sequence: cool the space, then extinguish the stuff.

6. [common] (text) The fire darkens down and the room goes to steam and black. The
   temptation is to keep flowing. When do you shut down, and what are you listening and
   looking for in the pause?
   > Model answer: Shut down once the glow is gone and the room's heat has clearly
   > dropped — then pause and read: listen for the crackle of remaining fire, watch
   > whether the smoke keeps lifting (winning) or re-darkens (fire's still eating
   > somewhere), feel the heat trend. Continuous flow past knockdown wastes water,
   > wrecks visibility with steam, and drowns the feedback the fire is giving you.
   > Flow, pause, read, repeat.

## Stage: After the Knock

7. [common] (text) Knockdown is done. Name what happens in this room in the next ten
   minutes, in order, and why "the fire's out" is a rookie sentence.
   > Model answer: Hydraulic overhaul of the obvious hot spots; open the mattress and
   > cushions (fires live inside furniture); check the walls, ceiling bays, and closet
   > for extension — hand on the wall, TIC as a guide, hook where it's suspicious;
   > confirm the window and door state for ventilation; and a secondary look after the
   > steam clears. "The fire's out" is rookie because the visible fire is out —
   > rekindles come from the fire you didn't open up after.

8. [common] (text) Your officer asks the teaching question on the ride home: "If that same
   room had been in the attached garage, what changes about the first line?" Answer it.
   > Model answer: Nearly everything gets heavier: garages hold cars, fuel, and dense
   > synthetic storage — a hotter, faster fire that argues for the 2½" or at least a
   > second line; the house-garage door becomes the critical control point (keep it
   > closed, fight to keep the fire out of the house); and the overhead door itself is
   > a hazard — springs fail in fire and the door can drop behind a crew. Same street,
   > same building, different fire — line selection starts with the fuel, not the
   > address.
