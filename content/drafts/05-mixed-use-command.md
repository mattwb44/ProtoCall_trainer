You are the officer of the first-due engine at 03:15: heavy fire in a corner
convenience store with two floors of apartments above, ordinary (brick-and-joist)
construction, hydrant on the corner. Cars are parked out front; some apartment
windows have lights on. A ladder and two more engines are on the assignment.

## Stage: First Five Minutes

1. [Officer] (text) Give your complete initial radio report, word for word, as you would
   transmit it.
   > Model answer: Expected elements, in a fluent transmission: unit on scene; building
   > (3-story ordinary, mixed-use, corner lot); conditions (heavy fire, floor 1
   > commercial, occupied apartments above); strategy (offensive for the residential
   > floors); action ("stretching a 2½" to the store"); resource request (working fire /
   > second alarm — occupied above a store fire at 3 AM is automatically more than one
   > alarm); command established with a name. Graded on completeness and decisiveness,
   > not phrasing.

2. [common] (text) Heavy fire in the store, unknown occupants above. What is the incident
   priority order here, and what does it make the first line do?
   > Model answer: Life safety on floors 2 and 3 drives everything. The first line goes
   > to the store fire not because the store matters but because that fire owns the only
   > interior stairs — water on the fire is what keeps the residential floors searchable.
   > Confinement of the fire below IS the rescue plan; a first line stretched anywhere
   > else abandons the people above it.

3. [Engineer] (text) You have the corner hydrant and a whole assignment coming in behind
   you. How do you position, and what do you leave for the ladder?
   > Model answer: Take the hydrant with a forward lay or have the next engine feed you —
   > but position past or short of the address so the front of the building is left open:
   > the ladder needs the corner scrub for the upper-floor windows, and this is exactly
   > the fire where aerial rescues happen. A perfectly supplied engine parked where the
   > aerial belongs is a net negative.

4. [Firefighter] (text) While the line is stretched, a man leans out a third-floor window
   yelling that the hallway is full of smoke. What do you tell him, and what do you
   report?
   > Model answer: Tell him to stay at the window, close the apartment door, and keep low
   > — he is safer sheltering at a window we can ladder than in a smoke-filled hallway.
   > Report: "Command, visible occupant, floor 3, side Alpha, sheltering in place, needs
   > a ladder." A calm instruction plus an exact location turns a panicking jumper into a
   > routine ladder rescue.

## Stage: Building the Fireground

5. [Officer] (text) Second alarm is arriving: two engines, a ladder, a rescue, a chief en
   route. Assign every company and say what you're deliberately NOT doing yet.
   > Model answer: Strong version: Engine 2 water supply + backup 2½" behind the first
   > line; Ladder 1 splits — outside team ladders the visible occupant and the front,
   > inside team forces and searches floor 2; Rescue takes floor 3 search; Engine 3
   > stretches to the interior stairs to protect the search above. Not yet: ventilation
   > of the upper floors (uncoordinated openings feed the store fire up the walls) and
   > overhaul of the store. Every company gets a task, a location, and an objective.

6. [common] (text) The chief arrives and takes command from you. What does a good transfer
   of command sound like from your side?
   > Model answer: A structured handoff, face to face or by radio: conditions (fire
   > knocked in the store, primary underway on 2, started on 3), actions (who is where,
   > doing what), needs (one more truck for the roof, EMS for the occupant coming down),
   > and safety notes (ordinary construction — fire may be in the joist channels and
   > cockloft). Then you take an assignment. Command transferred without a CAN-style
   > picture isn't a transfer, it's an abandonment.

7. [Engineer] (text) Command asks for a status on water. You're flowing the 2½", a backup
   line, and the ladder wants its pipe "just in case." Can the corner hydrant do it, and
   what do you tell command?
   > Model answer: Do the arithmetic out loud: 2½" ≈ 250 GPM, backup ≈ 150–250, aerial
   > master stream ≈ 500–1,000 — the pipe roughly doubles-to-triples demand, and one
   > hydrant on an old corner main may not carry it. Tell command the current lines are
   > secure, the pipe needs its own supply, and name the hydrant the next engine should
   > take. "We'll find out when it drops" is not a water plan.

## Stage: The Long Middle

8. [Officer] (text) Primary searches are clear, fire is knocked, and companies are asking
   to open up. In ordinary construction, where has this fire gone that nobody has seen,
   and how do you task the check?
   > Model answer: Vertically in the joist channels and pipe chases from the store, and
   > laterally in the common cockloft under the roof — ordinary construction moves fire
   > in voids that look like clean walls. Task it explicitly: a company per floor opening
   > ceilings and bays above the store's footprint, thermal imaging as a guide but not as
   > proof, roof crew checks the cockloft at the scuttle. Announce that no one leaves
   > until every floor reports checked.

9. [common] (text) It's 05:30. Crews have been working two hours; the fire is out; twelve
   residents are displaced on the sidewalk. Name what still has to be managed before this
   incident is actually over.
   > Model answer: Rehab and air management for crews who've been in bottles twice;
   > accountability sweep before any company is released; fire watch or a re-inspection
   > plan for the voids; the displaced residents handed to Red Cross or the city, not left
   > on a curb; utilities secured; the origin area preserved for investigation. Command
   > presence at hour two looks like logistics — the incident ends administratively, not
   > when the fire goes out.
