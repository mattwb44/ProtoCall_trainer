title: Narrow Street, Wires Down — Where Does the Truck Go?
category: Fireground
subcategory: Residential
objective_primary: Apparatus Placement
objective_secondary: Command Presence
difficulty: Standard
duration_min: 20
building_type: 2.5-story wood-frame duplexes, narrow one-way street, parked cars both sides

Reported fire, 21:20, on a one-way residential street barely wide enough for one rig
between parked cars. You're the engine chauffeur, first-due, with a ladder thirty
seconds behind you. Dispatch updates: caller now reports fire on the second floor and
wires arcing in front of the house.

## Stage: The Approach

1. [Engineer] (text) You're a block out. What do you slow down and decide BEFORE
   committing to this street, and why is this the least reversible decision of the fire?
   > Model answer: Everything about position: where the hydrant is, where the ladder
   > must go, whether you can even pass the address, and where the arcing wires are.
   > On a one-way street with parked cars, the first rig in defines the geometry for
   > every rig behind it — a bad spot can't be fixed, because you can't back out past
   > the assignment stacking up behind you. Slow is smooth; the extra fifteen seconds
   > of looking buys the whole fireground.

2. [common] (text) Wires are arcing across the front of the fire building. What does that
   do to apparatus placement and to firefighter movement, immediately?
   > Model answer: The wire's fall zone — pole to pole, plus slack — is now a no-park,
   > no-walk corridor until the utility kills it. Rigs stop short of it, lines are
   > stretched around it, ladders don't go up inside it, and someone is assigned to
   > mark it and say it on the radio. Energized wire on a wet street has killed more
   > than one firefighter who was looking at the fire instead.

3. [Engineer] (text) The ladder is behind you and this street fits one rig at the curb
   line. Engine takes the front, or engine yields it? Make the call and defend it.
   > Model answer: Yield it — on a street this tight, the general rule holds: the truck
   > gets the building, the engine gets the water. Fire on the second floor of a 2.5-story
   > means the aerial or ground ladders off the truck may be the rescue and the roof
   > access; your line stretches fine from a hundred feet away, the aerial doesn't.
   > Pull past the address (nose past, if the wires allow) so the ladder can spot the
   > front, and take the hydrant position.

4. [Officer] (text) Give your initial report including the placement and wire problems, so
   the rest of the assignment sets up correctly without being told twice.
   > Model answer: Building, conditions, strategy, plus the fireground geometry:
   > "one-way street, single-lane access, wires down and arcing side Alpha — ladder takes
   > the front, engine is past the building on the hydrant, all other companies stage off
   > the street until assigned, utility requested emergency response." A first report
   > that describes the street stops three rigs from burying each other in a dead end.

## Stage: Building the Puzzle

5. [common] (text) The second engine radios "coming down the street behind the ladder."
   Why is that wrong, and what should they do instead?
   > Model answer: The street is now full — one more rig in the slot means nobody
   > repositions, and if the ladder needs to adjust or EMS needs access, the lane is
   > gone. Second engine belongs at the hydrant end feeding the first engine, or staged
   > at the intersection, engine facing out. Uncommitted apparatus is a resource;
   > committed-and-stuck apparatus is furniture.

6. [Firefighter] (text) The ladder can't reach the rear of the duplex, and there's a
   report of someone at a rear second-floor window. What's the placement answer when the
   apparatus can't be the answer?
   > Model answer: Ground ladders — this is exactly why trucks carry them. Two
   > firefighters hump a 28-footer down the exposure side or through the adjacent yard
   > to the rear window. Announce it so command knows the rear is laddered. The aerial's
   > reach ends where the building's geometry says; the ladder company's job doesn't.

7. [Engineer] (text) The hydrant you took is dead — no water on the wrench. Nearest
   alternative is around the corner, 400 feet. Your engine is nose-in on a one-way
   street. Solve it.
   > Model answer: Don't move the engine — you can't, and you don't need to. Radio the
   > problem immediately, and have the staged second engine take the good hydrant and
   > lay 400 feet of supply to you (reverse or forward as geometry dictates), or relay-
   > pump if friction loss demands it. You're on tank water until it lands, so the
   > attack crew hears "tank water only" now, not when it runs out.

## Stage: When the Fire Changes the Plan

8. [Officer] (text) Fire extends and command declares defensive. Every rig is positioned
   for an offensive interior fire on a street with no room. What has to be re-examined,
   and what's the honest answer about some of it?
   > Model answer: Defensive means collapse zones — and the honest answer is the rigs
   > inside the building's height-and-a-half perimeter mostly cannot move on this street.
   > Re-examine which CAN reposition (staged units, EMS), pull crews and portable
   > equipment out of the zone even where apparatus must stay, and set master streams
   > from the corners — flanking positions — rather than the unmovable front. Placement
   > errors get paid for at strategy changes; the debrief lesson is that first-five-
   > minutes spotting should already have priced in "what if this goes defensive."

9. [common] (text) In the post-incident review, name the three placement decisions from
   this fire with the longest consequences, in order.
   > Model answer: (1) The engine yielding the front to the ladder — it determined
   > whether elevated rescue and roof work were possible at all. (2) Stopping short of
   > the arcing wires — a life-safety line nothing else on the fireground could undo.
   > (3) Keeping the second engine uncommitted — it was the slack that absorbed the dead
   > hydrant. Good placement is invisible when it works; these are the three that would
   > have been visible for the wrong reasons.
