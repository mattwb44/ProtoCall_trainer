A teaching scenario for newer members. You arrive at a single-story ranch house at
10:30 with smoke visible from the front door and one window. The scenario walks
through the four attributes of smoke — volume, velocity, density, and color — one
decision at a time. Untracked: everyone answers everything.

## Stage: The Four Attributes

1. [common] (text) Name the four attributes of smoke reading, and say in one line what
   each one tells you.
   > Model answer: Volume (how much of the building the fire has pressurized — how big
   > the event is), velocity (pressure — how hot the fire is pushing, and whether the
   > flow is heat-driven or just drifting), density (how much fuel is in the smoke —
   > thick smoke is unburned fuel that can ignite), and color (what stage and what's
   > burning — from white/light gray early or steam, through brown structural wood,
   > to black high-energy synthetic fuel).

2. [common] (text) From the front door: a large VOLUME of smoke, but drifting out slowly.
   From the window: less smoke, but pushing hard and turbulent. Which opening is closer
   to the fire, and why?
   > Model answer: The window — velocity beats volume for locating the fire. Fast,
   > turbulent smoke has heat right behind it pushing; slow, lazy smoke has traveled
   > and cooled, meaning the door is far from the seat and just draining the building.
   > Follow the pressure: the fire is near where the smoke moves like it's angry.

3. [common] (text) The smoke from the window is so thick you can't see into the room at
   all — dense, black, oily-looking. What is dense black smoke actually made of, and why
   do instructors call it "fuel"?
   > Model answer: Unburned products of incomplete combustion — carbon, aerosolized
   > hydrocarbons off synthetic contents — produced because the fire is too rich or
   > too starved of air to burn cleanly. It's called fuel because it IS fuel: given
   > temperature and air it ignites, which is what flashover and smoke explosions are.
   > Dense black smoke filling a space means you're inside the fire's gas tank.

## Stage: Watching It Change

4. [common] (text) Over two minutes, the window's smoke goes from black to yellowish-gray,
   and the flow starts to PULSE — pushing, pausing, pushing. Your instructor would call
   this the most important read of the day. What is the fire telling you?
   > Model answer: It's running out of air: yellowish-gray from a hot fire and pulsing
   > flow mean the fire has become ventilation-limited and the building is breathing —
   > pressure building and easing as combustion surges and starves. This fire is
   > primed to react violently to any new opening. The read matters most because it
   > changes what YOUR actions will do: from here, opening the building is what
   > determines the next sixty seconds.

5. [common] (text) A senior firefighter points at the smoke hugging the ground across the
   front lawn on a cool, humid morning and says "careful with that one." Why might
   ground-hugging smoke NOT mean what the textbook says — and what's the honest rule
   about single indicators?
   > Model answer: Smoke drops to the ground either because it's cooled (traveled far,
   > or a small smoldering fire) or because weather is holding it down — cool, humid,
   > still mornings flatten smoke regardless of the fire's state. One attribute alone
   > can lie. The rule: read all four attributes together, over time, against the
   > building; a single indicator is a hint, a converging set is a conclusion.

6. [common] (text) Compare what you'd expect to see from this same fire if the house were
   a big-box store instead of a ranch. Why does building size change the meaning of
   identical smoke?
   > Model answer: The same fire in a big-box would show almost nothing — a large volume
   > dilutes and cools smoke, so faint, lazy smoke from a big building can mean a LARGE
   > fire deep inside, while the identical wisp from a ranch house means a small one.
   > Smoke reading is always smoke-versus-building: judge the smoke against how much
   > building it had to fill before it showed itself.

## Stage: Calling It

7. [common] (text) Put it together: large volume, high velocity at the window, dense,
   turning yellow-gray and pulsing, in a small ranch house. State where the fire is,
   what stage it's in, and what one thing must be true before anyone opens the door.
   > Model answer: A significant contents fire in or near the room behind the window,
   > past free-burning and now ventilation-limited, with the whole (small) house
   > pressurized — a fire that will accelerate the moment it gets air. Before the door
   > opens: a charged line ready to flow immediately, because the opening starts a
   > clock. Stating fire location, stage, and the vent-limited warning is a complete
   > smoke-read radio report.

8. [common] (text) Last question, and it's the habit that makes the skill: smoke reading
   isn't a snapshot at arrival. When are the other moments on a fireground you should
   deliberately re-read the smoke, and what are you looking for at each?
   > Model answer: Continuously — but deliberately at every event that changes the
   > fire's air or water: after any door or window opens (did velocity jump — did we
   > feed it), when the line hits the fire (white steam and slowing smoke = water is
   > winning; no change = you're not on the seat), after ventilation (is the exhaust
   > doing its job), and any time smoke color darkens or flow turns pulsing (retreat-
   > level warning). The fire announces every change of state through its smoke; the
   > skill is remembering to keep listening.
