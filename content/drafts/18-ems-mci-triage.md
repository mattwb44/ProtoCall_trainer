A transit bus swerved and rolled onto its side on a rural highway at 07:40. Dispatch
reports "multiple injuries, number unknown." You are the officer on the first
ambulance to arrive: the bus is on its right side, some passengers have
self-extricated and sit or wander on the shoulder, cries are audible from inside.
Next units: an engine in four minutes, two ambulances in ten, more on request.

## Stage: The Hardest Discipline

1. [Officer] (text) You are two EMS providers looking at maybe two dozen patients. What
   is your crew's job in the first ten minutes — and what is it NOT, no matter how loud
   the cries from the bus are?
   > Model answer: Command and triage — establish command, do a size-up lap, count and
   > sort patients, and build the incident that the arriving wave will plug into. NOT
   > patient care: two providers treating one victim helps one person; two providers
   > triaging and organizing helps all of them. Walking past treatable patients to
   > finish triage is the most unnatural act in EMS and the entire point of MCI
   > doctrine — the first crew that starts treating has decided the other twenty
   > patients can wait.

2. [Officer] (text) Give your initial radio report for this scene, and state the resource
   request. What's the rule for how much to order when the count is still unknown?
   > Model answer: "MCI declared: transit bus rollover, [location], estimating 20–25
   > patients, unknown entrapments, command established, staging at [location]."
   > Request big and round: mass-casualty response — five-plus transport units, two
   > engines for extrication and manpower, EMS supervisor, air medical on standby,
   > police for the highway. The rule: order for the worst plausible count, because
   > over-ordered units get canceled in one sentence while under-ordering is paid for
   > in patient outcomes. You cannot catch up to an MCI you started behind.

3. [common] (text) You start triage at the walking wounded on the shoulder. Why start
   there — and what single instruction sorts them fastest?
   > Model answer: "Everyone who can hear me and walk, move to the grass by my
   > vehicle" — one sentence just triaged everyone who moves as minor/green: walking
   > and following commands screens airway, circulation, and mentation at once. It
   > also physically gathers them (greens scatter, and scattered greens become
   > repeat patients or traffic casualties) and clears your sight lines to the people
   > who DIDN'T move — who are now your triage priority.

## Stage: Sorting the Bus

4. [common] (text) Inside the bus: your partner triages with 30–60 seconds per patient.
   Describe what a rapid triage assessment actually checks, and what "immediate/red"
   findings look like — using whatever triage system your department runs.
   > Model answer: The universal skeleton (START and its relatives): can they walk; if
   > not — breathing? Not breathing after airway repositioning = deceased/black.
   > Breathing over ~30/min, no radial pulse or cap refill over 2 seconds, or can't
   > follow simple commands = immediate/red. Everything else = delayed/yellow. The
   > only treatments during triage: open an airway, stop massive bleeding
   > (tourniquet), then MOVE ON — triage is sorting, and every extra minute with one
   > patient is a minute of no one sorting the rest.

5. [Firefighter] (text) The engine arrives and the crew heads for the bus with tools.
   As triage officer, what do you need from them FIRST, before extrication — and what
   patient-movement trap does an eager engine crew fall into?
   > Model answer: First: stabilize the bus and make access survivable — then
   > extrication PRIORITIZED BY TRIAGE: reds out first, and you tell them which
   > patients those are. The trap: pulling patients in seating order or easiest-first,
   > which fills your treatment area with yellows while reds die in the back rows.
   > Extrication at an MCI is a resource that triage allocates, not a race the tools
   > run on their own.

6. [Officer] (text) Ten minutes in: 4 red, 7 yellow, 11 green, 1 black. Two ambulances
   have arrived and more are coming in a trickle. Design the flow — treatment area,
   transport decisions, and who goes first in what.
   > Model answer: Treatment area on the grass, uphill/upwind, marked by category, with
   > arriving providers assigned to it (not to the bus — extrication has enough hands).
   > Transport officer designated — one person owns who leaves in what. Reds go first,
   > two-per-ambulance if they can be managed, to be DISTRIBUTED across receiving
   > hospitals rather than swamping the closest ED; air medical takes the worst red to
   > the trauma center. Yellows follow, greens go last and by bus or van if available.
   > The scene is a pump: triage feeds treatment, treatment feeds transport, and the
   > transport officer is the valve.

## Stage: The Long Tail

7. [common] (text) A green-tagged woman in the grass suddenly vomits and becomes hard to
   wake. What does this teach about triage tags, and what must the system around the
   greens include?
   > Model answer: Triage is a snapshot, not a diagnosis — head injuries, internal
   > bleeds, and shock evolve, and greens are just patients whose deterioration hasn't
   > happened yet. She's re-triaged red and moved NOW. The system: greens get a
   > dedicated minder doing periodic re-checks, and every category gets re-triaged as
   > the scene matures. The MCI that ends badly in court and in conscience is the one
   > where a green died in the grass with no one assigned to notice.

8. [Officer] (text) An hour later: everyone transported, the highway still closed, your
   crews scattered across three hospitals and the scene. What does closing out an MCI
   properly require — for the incident and for the people who worked it?
   > Model answer: Accountability first — every patient tracked to a destination (the
   > tag numbers and transport log reconciled), every responder accounted for.
   > Equipment recovered and restocked because the next call doesn't wait; the scene
   > released to police for investigation; a patient count and hospital distribution
   > reported up the chain. For the people: a real demobilization — brief defusing,
   > honest word about what they saw, and the CISM/peer-support offer made concretely,
   > not as a pamphlet. MCIs leave marks on providers; closing the incident includes
   > opening that door.
