objective_secondary: Command Presence

Apartment fire at 15:20 on a dry, windy afternoon: heavy fire on the third floor of
Building B in a three-building garden apartment complex, wind pushing flames and
embers directly toward Building C, thirty feet downwind. Dozens of residents are
self-evacuating. You are the battalion chief arriving to take command; a full first
alarm (three engines, two trucks, a rescue) is on scene or arriving.

## Stage: The Opening Ledger

1. [common] (text) Name every problem on this fireground competing for companies right
   now, then rank them. What principle orders the list?
   > Model answer: Fire attack in B, primary search of B, exposure protection of C
   > (ember and radiant), evacuation/search of C, evacuation management of the crowd,
   > water supply, and utilities. Ranking flows from life safety weighted by time
   > pressure: search of B and protection-plus-clearing of C outrank pure fire attack —
   > but attack in B is also life safety, because the fire is what's manufacturing all
   > the other problems. Resource management starts with admitting the list is longer
   > than the resources.

2. [Officer] (text) You have six companies. Assign all six, and state what's explicitly
   UNSTAFFED and accepted as risk.
   > Model answer: A defensible split: E1 attack line to B floor 3; T1 primary search
   > B; E2 supply + exposure lines on C's face; T2 clears and searches C (downwind =
   > second fire building in waiting); Rescue as RIT-plus-search-surge; E3 second supply
   > and backup line in B. Unstaffed and accepted: Building A (upwind — checked, not
   > staffed), salvage, overhaul, crowd management beyond police. Command's job is
   > choosing the uncovered risks out loud rather than pretending six companies cover
   > nine problems.

3. [common] (text) Why should your next radio transmission after the assignments be a
   request — and for how much?
   > Model answer: Because the gap between the problem list and the staffing is already
   > visible, and resources requested now arrive in ten minutes while resources
   > requested when needed arrive late. Wind-driven fire, exposure building, mass
   > evacuation: second alarm minimum, and striking a third for a complex this size is
   > cheap insurance — extra companies can stage; missing companies can't be conjured.
   > Order early, stage aggressively, release honestly.

## Stage: The Ledger Moves

4. [Officer] (text) T2 reports Building C's breezeway is taking fire from embers in the
   mulch and a balcony is smoking. E2's lines aren't stretched yet. What do you shuffle,
   and what does the shuffle cost?
   > Model answer: This is the pivot point: C igniting doubles the incident. Surge on C
   > — divert E3's backup line (or the Rescue) to knock the spot fires and wet the face
   > NOW, accept the cost stated plainly: B's attack loses its backup line for several
   > minutes, meaning B's interior crews operate on thinner margin and should be told
   > so. Every reassignment on a stretched fireground is a loan from somewhere; say the
   > cost on the radio so the lender knows.

5. [Engineer] (text) Two engines are now flowing serious water and a third is asking where
   to get supply. The complex has two hydrants on the same dead-end main. What's the
   supply-side resource problem, and the fix?
   > Model answer: Both hydrants share one main — the second engine to connect may steal
   > pressure from the first, and a dead-end main can't feed a multi-building defensive
   > fight. Fix: split the sources — send the third engine out of the complex to a
   > hydrant on a different main (check the map, don't guess) and relay or lay in.
   > Water is a resource with its own logistics; treating hydrants as infinite is how
   > two working fires become one unfought one.

6. [Firefighter] (text) You're on the exposure line on C when residents start re-entering
   Building C for pets and medications. You can't leave the line. What's the resource-
   correct action?
   > Model answer: Radio it as a task for command, not a problem you absorb: "Command,
   > exposure line — civilians re-entering C, need bodies for scene control side Delta."
   > Leaving a flowing exposure line to shepherd residents un-defends the building
   > they're re-entering. Naming a task you cannot do is a contribution, not a failure;
   > freelancing off an assigned position is how command's ledger stops matching
   > reality.

## Stage: Sustained Operations

7. [Officer] (text) Second and third alarms have arrived: you now have twelve companies,
   and the span-of-control math has broken. Reorganize the fireground.
   > Model answer: Divisions and groups: Division B (interior/attack), Division C
   > (exposure), a Search Group across both buildings, Water Supply Group, Staging with
   > a manager, RIT maintained. Command talks to five supervisors, not twelve companies.
   > This is resource management's second phase — the constraint stops being companies
   > and becomes command bandwidth, and the org chart is how you buy more of it.

8. [common] (text) An hour in: fire in B is knocked, C is saved, crews are on their second
   bottles in 90-degree heat. What resource is now the scarcest on this fireground, and
   what does managing it look like?
   > Model answer: Human endurance. Rehab becomes a tactical function: formal rehab with
   > EMS monitoring, crew rotations enforced by command (crews don't self-select out —
   > they get relieved), fresh third-alarm companies cycled into overhaul, and worked
   > companies released or staged. The fires you lose at hour two are firefighters:
   > cardiac events and heat casualties are resource-management failures wearing medical
   > costumes.

9. [Officer] (text) The city EOC calls: they can open a shelter but need a number for
   displaced residents, and the press is asking when units will clear. Why are these
   command problems, and what's your answer to each?
   > Model answer: Because the incident's resources include the city's, and feeding the
   > EOC accurate numbers is how residents get beds — task a company or a PIO-type role
   > to count units uninhabitable and heads displaced, and give the EOC a real figure
   > with a caveat. For clearing: an honest horizon ("overhaul and fire watch into the
   > evening, most companies released within two hours") rather than optimism. Resource
   > management ends with giving resources back — to the department and to the city —
   > in good order.
