Dispatched at 09:25 to a senior center for a 72-year-old woman "acting confused."
Staff meet you at the door: she was fine at breakfast, then about twenty minutes ago
her speech got slurred and she "stopped using her right hand." She's seated,
awake, looking anxious. Untracked: everyone answers everything.

(Reviewer note: candidate EMS objectives for the vocabulary — "Patient Assessment",
"Neuro Emergencies" — pending site-admin addition.)

## Stage: Recognition

1. [common] (text) The dispatch said "confused"; the story says something else. What are
   you thinking from the doorway, and why does the phrase "last known well" need to be
   nailed down before almost anything else?
   > Model answer: Sudden focal deficits — slurred speech, one-sided weakness — is a
   > stroke presentation, not confusion. "Last known well" is the anchor of every
   > downstream decision: thrombolytics and thrombectomy eligibility are measured from
   > the last moment she was DEFINITELY normal, not from when symptoms were noticed.
   > "Fine at breakfast, changed twenty minutes ago" with a staff witness is a gift —
   > get the exact time and the witness's name in writing before the story blurs.

2. [common] (text) Perform your stroke screen out loud — the components of whatever
   scale you use (Cincinnati/FAST or your protocol's), what a positive looks like, and
   the one common mimic you must rule out on scene.
   > Model answer: Face — ask her to smile, look for droop; Arm — both arms up, eyes
   > closed, watch for drift (her right arm will tell the story); Speech — a repeated
   > phrase, listen for slurring or wrong words. Any one positive puts stroke on the
   > table; she likely shows two or three. The must-check mimic: glucose — hypoglycemia
   > produces perfect stroke imitations, and a fingerstick takes thirty seconds. A
   > "stroke" that's actually sugar is fixed in the ambulance; a real stroke sent down
   > the sugar path loses its window.

3. [common] (text) Her right arm drifts, her smile droops right, and she says "I can't
   make the words right." Glucose is 108. What ELSE do you need from the staff before
   you leave — the three pieces of history the stroke team will demand?
   > Model answer: (1) Medications — especially anticoagulants (warfarin, the DOACs):
   > blood thinners change thrombolytic eligibility instantly; (2) recent surgery,
   > falls, bleeding, or prior stroke/brain bleed — the contraindication list; (3) her
   > baseline — was any of this deficit pre-existing? Plus next-of-kin contact, because
   > consent conversations happen fast at the hospital. Thirty seconds with a staff
   > member who knows her beats ten minutes of the ED calling around later.

## Stage: The Transport Decision

4. [common] (text) On-scene time for a stroke patient: what's the target, what actually
   belongs in it, and name two things crews commonly do on scene that should happen in
   the truck instead.
   > Model answer: Aim for ten minutes or less on scene. What belongs: the screen,
   > glucose, last-known-well, the history grab, and packaging. What gets dragged into
   > the truck: the full 12-lead workup and IV attempts — both can happen rolling, and
   > neither changes the destination. A stroke scene time is a discipline problem, not
   > a knowledge problem; every minute on scene is brain.

5. [common] (text) Two hospitals: a community ED eight minutes away, and a comprehensive
   stroke center twenty-two minutes away. Her deficits are significant. Walk the
   destination decision — what does severity have to do with it?
   > Model answer: Severity is the fork: severe deficits suggest a possible large-
   > vessel occlusion, which needs thrombectomy — available at the comprehensive
   > center, NOT the community ED, and interfacility transfer burns an hour or more.
   > Most modern protocols run a severity scale (RACE, LAMS, or similar): score high
   > and the extra fourteen minutes to the right hospital saves net hours of brain.
   > Score low, closest appropriate wins. Know your protocol's answer — but the graded
   > skill is weighing minutes-to-door against capability-behind-the-door.

6. [common] (text) Compose your stroke alert notification to the receiving hospital —
   the sentence-long version that mobilizes a team before you arrive.
   > Model answer: "Stroke alert: 72-year-old female, last known well 09:05, right-side
   > weakness and slurred speech, [scale] positive/score X, glucose normal, on [no]
   > anticoagulants, ETA 20 minutes." Last-known-well and the anticoagulant answer are
   > the two facts that let the team stage CT and pharmacy before the doors open —
   > a good prenotification routinely cuts door-to-treatment time by double-digit
   > minutes. That radio call is a treatment.

## Stage: En Route and Handoff

7. [common] (text) Ten minutes out, her speech clears and the arm comes back —
   completely. She says she feels fine and asks if you can just turn around. What
   changed clinically, and what changes operationally?
   > Model answer: Clinically this now looks like a TIA — and a TIA is a stroke warning
   > shot, with a substantial short-term risk of the real thing; the resolved deficit
   > does NOT downgrade the destination or the urgency. Operationally: document the
   > deficit you SAW (your exam is now the only evidence it happened), note resolution
   > time, continue to the stroke-capable facility, and keep the alert active or update
   > it per protocol — the workup that prevents next week's completed stroke starts
   > tonight. Kindly, the answer to "turn around" is no.

8. [common] (text) At the handoff, the resident asks, "Why does EMS matter so much in
   stroke? The hospital does all the treatment." Give the answer this call just
   demonstrated.
   > Model answer: Because every irreversible decision happened before the doors:
   > recognizing that "confusion" was focal deficit, pinning last-known-well with a
   > witness, ruling out the glucose mimic, choosing the twenty-two-minute hospital
   > over the eight-minute one, and the prenotification that had CT waiting. The
   > hospital delivers the drug and the catheter; EMS decides whether the patient
   > arrives inside the window, at the right building, with the facts that make
   > treatment possible. Time is brain, and most of the clock runs in the ambulance.
