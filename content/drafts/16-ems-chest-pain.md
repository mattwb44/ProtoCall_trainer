BLS-first EMS fundamentals. Dispatched at 11:15 for a 58-year-old male with chest
pain at a pharmacy. You arrive to find him seated on a bench, pale, a hand on his
chest, employees hovering. ALS is dispatched behind you, eight minutes out.
Untracked: everyone answers everything.

## Stage: First Contact

1. [common] (text) Describe your general impression from the doorway — what are you
   assessing before you say a word, and what about THIS presentation already worries you?
   > Model answer: The across-the-room read: skin (pale — poor perfusion), position
   > (seated, guarded, hand on chest — classic cardiac posture), work of breathing,
   > mentation (tracking you or not). Pale plus diaphoretic plus a hand on the chest in
   > a 58-year-old male is a cardiac presentation until proven otherwise; the doorway
   > impression has already put ACS at the top of the differential before the first
   > question.

2. [common] (text) Structure your first two minutes with this patient: what you ask, what
   your partner does, and why the sequence is parallel rather than serial.
   > Model answer: You take the story — OPQRST for the pain, SAMPLE for the background —
   > while your partner takes vitals, gets him on oxygen if indicated, and sets up the
   > monitor/AED nearby. Parallel because cardiac time is muscle: the interview,
   > vitals, and preparation for deterioration should finish together, not in a queue.
   > Talk to him by name; a calm crew measurably calms the patient, and anxiety feeds
   > cardiac workload.

3. [common] (text) He describes "pressure, like a weight," radiating to his jaw, started
   40 minutes ago while walking, 7/10, some nausea. Which of those details push toward
   cardiac, and what one medication question must you ask before anyone reaches for
   nitroglycerin?
   > Model answer: Nearly all of them: pressure quality, jaw radiation, exertional
   > onset, nausea — a textbook anginal cluster. The mandatory question: has he taken
   > any erectile-dysfunction medication (sildenafil and relatives) in the last 24–48
   > hours — nitro plus those drugs can drop his pressure catastrophically. Ask it
   > plainly and privately; embarrassment has killed patients.

## Stage: Treatment and the Clock

4. [common] (text) Vitals: BP 148/92, pulse 96 irregular, RR 20, SpO2 96% on room air.
   Walk through your BLS treatment decisions from here — including what you do NOT do.
   > Model answer: Aspirin, chewed, if no allergy or bleeding contraindication — the
   > one BLS drug proven to change AMI mortality. Nitro only per protocol (his own
   > prescription or medical direction), pressure permitting, ED-med question answered.
   > NOT: high-flow oxygen at 96% — supplemental O2 without hypoxia shows no benefit;
   > titrate to protocol, typically ≥94%. Keep him seated and still, monitor on,
   > reassess. Restraint is part of the treatment.

5. [common] (text) His pain "isn't that bad now" and he wants to drive himself to his
   doctor. He is alert and oriented. How do you handle the refusal conversation?
   > Model answer: He has capacity, so you persuade honestly rather than bluff:
   > improving pain doesn't unhappen a cardiac event, his rhythm is irregular, and the
   > place his symptoms get sorted out is an ED, arriving by ambulance — because if he
   > arrests, it happens next to a defibrillator and a crew. Enlist family by phone if
   > he'll allow it. If he still refuses: medical direction on the line, full-capacity
   > refusal documented, and a genuinely warm "call us back the second anything
   > changes." The best refusal paperwork is the transport you talked him into.

6. [common] (text) He agrees to transport. ALS is now four minutes out. Make the call:
   wait for the medics, start toward them, or transport BLS to the hospital ten minutes
   away? Defend your choice.
   > Model answer: The defensible default: begin moving — package him and either meet
   > ALS en route (a planned intercept) or continue in, since a stable-for-now cardiac
   > patient benefits more from a hospital than a parking lot. Waiting on scene adds
   > minutes and adds nothing he can't get moving. The honest exception: if he's
   > deteriorating, ALS coming TO you is faster than any intercept. The skill being
   > graded is that you made a time-based decision, out loud, rather than defaulting
   > to waiting.

## Stage: Deterioration

7. [common] (text) In the ambulance he suddenly slumps, unresponsive, agonal breathing.
   You're two minutes from the ALS intercept. First thirty seconds — go.
   > Model answer: This is an arrest until proven otherwise: check response and pulse
   > fast (ten seconds max), tell your partner to pull over safely, start compressions
   > hard and fast, pads on — analyze — shock if advised, and radio the intercept and
   > the hospital in one breath. A witnessed arrest with a defibrillator in reach is
   > the most survivable cardiac arrest there is; the outcome is decided by how little
   > time you waste being surprised.

8. [common] (text) Debrief: your officer asks what the transport decision teaches now
   that you know how the call ended. What's the honest lesson — and what's the wrong
   lesson?
   > Model answer: The honest lesson: cardiac patients are unstable BY NATURE, so every
   > choice — move early, pads pre-attached, partner in the back, intercept planned —
   > should assume the crash you can't predict; a witnessed arrest next to a ready AED
   > went as well as it can go. The wrong lesson: "we should have waited for ALS" —
   > hindsight doesn't change that moving toward definitive care with a monitor
   > attached was right on the information you had. Judge decisions by the information
   > at the time, not the outcome; that's what keeps debriefs honest.
