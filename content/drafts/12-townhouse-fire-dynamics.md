Fire in a middle-unit townhouse, 20:15: dark smoke pulsing from around the closed
front door and the first-floor window frames, black-stained glass, no visible flame.
Occupants report everyone out. Modern open-plan interior, synthetic furnishings.
Your engine is first-due; the truck is one minute behind.

## Stage: Reading the Fire's State

1. [common] (text) Pulsing smoke at the door seams, stained-black intact windows, no
   flame. Describe the fire's state in fire-dynamics terms, and what the building is one
   action away from.
   > Model answer: Ventilation-limited: the fire has consumed the oxygen in its
   > compartment and is idling fuel-rich — the pulsing is the pressure breathing of a
   > fire trying to inhale, and the black-stained glass says high heat with unburned
   > products condensed everywhere. The building is one opening away from rapid fire
   > growth: any door or window becomes an intake, and the fire will accelerate toward
   > flashover-level burning along whatever path connects new air to the seat.

2. [Officer] (text) Modern synthetics and open floor plans changed the timeline your
   senior members trained on. State what changed and what it means for the first five
   minutes of this fire.
   > Model answer: Synthetic fuels release heat several times faster than legacy
   > furnishings — flashover that once took many minutes can arrive in three or four —
   > and open plans remove the compartment walls that used to slow spread, so the
   > "room" that flashes is the whole first floor. For the first five minutes: assume
   > the fire is farther along than it looks, control every opening, and get water on
   > it before making entry the fire can exploit. Speed matters, but sequenced speed.

3. [Engineer] (text) There's a hose-accessible first-floor window with fire likely just
   inside it. Argue for or against hitting the fire through the window before the crew
   enters the front door.
   > Model answer: For — this is transitional attack, and the physics is settled:
   > a straight stream off the ceiling through the window for fifteen to thirty seconds
   > drops the compartment's temperature and resets the flashover clock without
   > "pushing fire" (properly applied streams don't). The crew then enters a cooled
   > building. Against applies only if the stream can't reach the seat or a known
   > victim lies in the steam path. Here: hit it, then go.

## Stage: Flow Path Discipline

4. [Firefighter] (text) You're forcing the front door for entry after the exterior knock.
   Everyone talks about "door control" — perform it in words: what exactly do you do with
   this door, and what are you preventing?
   > Model answer: Force it but keep it closed — a hand or tool on it, or a strap —
   > until the line is charged and the crew is masked and ready; then open it only as
   > far as the advance needs, with someone owning it the whole time. You're preventing
   > the door from becoming a full-time intake: an uncontrolled open front door feeds
   > the fire a highway of air, and the exhaust path it creates runs right over the
   > advancing crew.

5. [common] (text) Inside, first floor: heat is high but visibility is fair after the
   knock. As you advance, conditions suddenly worsen — smoke darkens, lowers, and starts
   moving fast over your heads toward the rear. No one opened anything on floor 1. What
   happened, and what does the smoke's speed tell you?
   > Model answer: Something opened somewhere else — a rear door, a failed window, or
   > the truck venting off-plan — and you are now IN the flow path: the fast dark smoke
   > overhead is the exhaust leg running from the fire to the new opening, and fire
   > follows exhaust. Fast smoke means a fire with a fresh air supply accelerating.
   > Flow, communicate, and either finish the knock immediately or get out of the
   > path's lane — under it is the worst place on the fireground.

6. [Officer] (text) You learn the truck opened the rear slider "to vent for you." Fix the
   coordination failure on the radio in one transmission, and state the rule that was
   broken.
   > Model answer: "All companies — no ventilation without command's order; rear slider
   > is open, attack is in the flow path, truck close it NOW until we're on the seat."
   > The rule: ventilation serves the attack and happens on the attack's timing —
   > opening behind a fire that isn't yet being hit with water doesn't relieve the
   > interior crew, it points the fire at them. Good intentions plus bad timing equals
   > tactical arson.

## Stage: The Upper Floors

7. [common] (text) Fire is knocked on floor 1. The stairwell to floors 2 and 3 is
   open-plan and full of hot smoke that is NOT lifting. In fire-dynamics terms, why isn't
   it clearing, and what makes stairwells uniquely dangerous after knockdown?
   > Model answer: Heat stratifies — the stairwell is a dead-end chimney holding a
   > buoyant reservoir of hot, fuel-rich gases with no exhaust above, so it can't lift.
   > It's dangerous because that reservoir is still within its flammable range: a
   > lingering pocket of fire or a fresh opening can ignite it, and stairwells
   > concentrate any ignition into a vertical blowtorch. Crews relax after knockdown;
   > the gas layer doesn't.

8. [Firefighter] (text) Ascending the stairs to check for extension, you feel heat
   INCREASING at the ceiling as you climb, and your TIC shows the gas layer overhead at
   several hundred degrees. What do you do before taking another step, and why does
   cooling the gases matter even with no flame visible?
   > Model answer: Pencil the gas layer — short bursts of straight stream into the
   > overhead — and watch the response before advancing. Gas cooling contracts the layer
   > and pulls it below its ignition energy; a several-hundred-degree fuel-rich layer
   > over your head is flashover's ingredients waiting for a spark. No visible flame
   > means it hasn't ignited YET — water spent on hot smoke above you is life insurance,
   > not waste.

9. [Officer] (text) Post-fire walkthrough with a new firefighter: she asks why the fire
   "waited" — big pulsing smoke on arrival but no fire showing until you made openings.
   Give her the fire-dynamics narrative of this incident, start to finish.
   > Model answer: The fire grew until it spent the townhouse's oxygen, then throttled
   > itself back to an idle — hot, fuel-rich, waiting for air; that's the pulsing you
   > saw. Every opening we made was a decision about feeding it: the exterior stream
   > cooled it before the front door gave it breath, door control rationed the intake,
   > and the uncoordinated slider showed exactly how fast it answers new air. Modern
   > fires are ventilation-limited by default — which means WE usually hold the
   > throttle, if we act like it.
