Automatic alarm upgraded to a working fire at a big-box home-goods store, 19:40,
just after closing. Light smoke throughout the retail floor, heavier toward the
stockroom at the rear — roughly 300 feet from the front doors. A manager believes
one employee may still be inside. Your crew is assigned to advance and locate the
fire; a search team follows.

## Stage: Before the Door

1. [common] (text) Your cylinder says 45 minutes on the sticker. In this building, how
   much of that is actually yours to use going IN — and what rule governs it?
   > Model answer: A third, at most — the working figure is: a third in, a third out,
   > a third in reserve for the emergency you didn't plan. And the sticker lies under
   > work: a firefighter advancing line under stress burns a "45-minute" bottle in
   > 20–25 minutes. In a 300-foot building, the point of no return is an air
   > calculation, not a distance — you turn around based on your gauge, not on whether
   > you found the fire.

2. [Officer] (text) Set the air discipline for your crew before entry: what do you brief,
   in one breath, at the door?
   > Model answer: "Everyone check and call your PSI now; lowest bottle sets the pace;
   > air checks every couple minutes or at every benchmark — I'll call them; we turn
   > around at [stated PSI], together, no discussion; anyone hits their low-air alarm,
   > we are already late." The turnaround number is set at the door, out loud, when
   > everyone can think — not improvised at the back of a smoke-filled warehouse.

3. [Firefighter] (text) Why is a hoseline or search rope non-negotiable for this entry,
   in air-management terms specifically?
   > Model answer: Because your way out is part of your air math. The exit calculation
   > assumes you can travel out as fast as you came in — true only if the route is a
   > rope or a hoseline you can follow by touch. Disoriented in 40,000 square feet of
   > racking, your "one-third out" becomes your reserve, then becomes your low-air
   > alarm, then becomes a mayday. The line isn't just water; it's the exit clock's
   > collateral.

## Stage: Deep Interior

4. [common] (text) Two hundred feet in, visibility drops to nothing and the crew is
   working hard around fallen stock. Your officer calls an air check: readings come back
   2,800 / 2,600 / 2,100 on 4,500 PSI bottles. What does that spread mean and what does
   the crew do?
   > Model answer: The 2,100 firefighter is the crew's bottle now — the crew's remaining
   > time is the lowest reading, and a 700 PSI spread says someone is working harder or
   > breathing worse, which is information in itself. At under half on the lowest bottle,
   > 200 feet deep with hard travel, you are at or past the decision point: report it and
   > start the exit conversation now. Crews don't run out of air at the same time; they
   > run out when the first member does.

5. [Firefighter] (text) You're the 2,100. Pride says keep quiet and breathe shallow.
   What do you actually do, and why is concealing your air reading the worst thing on
   this fireground?
   > Model answer: Call your number, loud and immediately. A concealed low bottle
   > converts a routine crew exit into a mayday with your name on it — RIT deploying
   > 250 feet into a big-box for a preventable out-of-air is the highest-risk operation
   > in the fire service. Air readings are crew data, not personal performance scores;
   > the culture that mocks the low bottle kills the quiet ones.

6. [Officer] (text) As you begin exiting, the search team radios they may have a viable
   victim near the stockroom and asks your crew to assist. Your lowest bottle is 1,900.
   Answer them.
   > Model answer: No — and say why on the radio: "unable, air, exiting." At 1,900 PSI
   > and 200-plus feet of travel, assisting means arriving with nothing to give and
   > adding two more bodies to the rescue problem. The assist goes to a fresh crew from
   > staging. Refusing a rescue task on air grounds feels unbearable and is exactly what
   > the reserve-third rule exists to make automatic.

## Stage: The Emergency Margin

7. [common] (text) On the way out, a rack collapse blocks the aisle your hoseline runs
   through. The crew is intact; bottles read 1,600 / 1,400 / 1,100. Walk through the
   first sixty seconds.
   > Model answer: Stop, breathe, get low, and transmit — this is a mayday-level report
   > even with everyone uninjured: location, crew intact, route blocked, air status.
   > Then air discipline becomes survival: stop working, slow every breath, kill any
   > effort that isn't finding the secondary route (follow the line backward under the
   > collapse, or crew search for the parallel aisle). The 1,100 bottle has maybe eight
   > calm minutes or four panicked ones — calm is a resource you choose.

8. [Firefighter] (text) Your low-air alarm activates while the crew works the blockage.
   What does that alarm actually mean — and what's the common fatal misreading of it?
   > Model answer: It means your reserve is OPEN — you are now spending the emergency
   > margin, roughly 25–33% of the bottle, and everything from here is borrowed. The
   > fatal misreading is treating it as "time to start leaving": the alarm is calibrated
   > for the exit you should have already made, not for beginning one through a collapse.
   > Report it, stay on the crew, and if a RIT bottle or filtered exit option reaches
   > you, take it without ego.

9. [Officer] (text) Everyone got out; the employee had left before your entry. In the
   debrief, the crew wants a rule of thumb for "how deep is too deep on one bottle" in
   big-box buildings. Give them one, with its reasoning.
   > Model answer: One honest version: if the fire or objective is more than ~150 feet
   > from your entry point, one bottle buys you travel and a few minutes of work — plan
   > relief crews, staged bottles, or don't go. Reasoning: 150 feet of low-visibility
   > travel each way plus work consumes two-thirds of real-world bottle time, leaving
   > exactly the reserve the rule demands. Big-box fires are air-supply operations that
   > happen to involve water.
