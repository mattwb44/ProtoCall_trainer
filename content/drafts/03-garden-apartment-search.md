Apartment fire, 23:50. On arrival: fire showing from a second-floor window of a
three-story garden apartment, moderate smoke in the open center stairwell. A resident
meets you screaming that "the kids in 2B are still inside" — 2B is the fire apartment.
You are the first-due company; the next unit is four minutes out.

## Stage: Arrival Decision

1. [common] (text) One company, a confirmed rescue in the fire apartment, and a fire
   that's growing. What gets your crew — the line or the search — and why?
   > Model answer: There is no universal answer, and that's the point: with a known
   > rescue, most doctrines put the first line between the fire and the victims or on the
   > fire itself, because water on the fire improves survivability everywhere at once.
   > A split (officer + firefighter to search off the line's protection, engineer to the
   > pump) is defensible with a crew of four. What's not defensible is searching deep in
   > the fire apartment with no water anywhere.

2. [Officer] (text) Give your initial radio report, including the rescue, and your
   assignment for the next-due company.
   > Model answer: "Three-story garden apartment, fire showing floor 2, confirmed
   > occupants trapped in the fire apartment, offensive, first line going to the fire
   > apartment, requesting the second alarm." Next-due gets search of the fire apartment
   > under the first line's protection — or the floor above if your crew reaches the
   > bedrooms first. Calling extra resources with a confirmed rescue is automatic, not
   > optional.

3. [Firefighter] (text) The resident says the kids' bedroom is "in the back, on the
   left." A second-floor rear window on that side is intact with no smoke behind it.
   What tactic does that suggest, and what's the first thing you do at that window?
   > Model answer: VEIS — the bedroom is a known point target and the window puts you
   > there without crossing the fire. First action after taking the glass and clearing
   > the sill: sweep and sound the floor below the sill, enter, and get to the door and
   > CLOSE IT before searching. The closed door isolates the room from the flow path;
   > skipping it turns your vent opening into an exhaust that pulls fire to you.

## Stage: Primary Search

4. [common] (text) Inside 2B the smoke is banked to a meter off the floor and hot. The
   line is flowing on the fire in the living room. How does the search team stay oriented
   in an apartment none of you have seen?
   > Model answer: Garden apartments repeat — 2B's layout is 1B's and 3B's, so someone
   > glancing into an uninvolved unit gives you the floor plan. Search oriented: one
   > member anchored to a wall or the line, sweeping crews working off that anchor,
   > bedrooms prioritized because it's midnight. Know your exit at all times — the window
   > you came in and the door you came past are both viable.

5. [Firefighter] (text) You find a child, unresponsive, in the second bedroom. The window
   is right there; the apartment door means passing the fire room with a knocked-but-live
   fire. Which way out, and what do you transmit?
   > Model answer: The window — you're in a VEIS position and a ground ladder or crew
   > should be at it; passing a live fire room with a victim trades a known exit for a
   > gamble. Transmit immediately: victim found, location, your exit route, and that the
   > search of the room is incomplete if it is — the next crew must know whether "one
   > child found" means the room is clear.

6. [Officer] (text) One child is out. The parent now says there may be a second child but
   is hysterical and uncertain. Fire is knocked but the apartment is punishing. How do you
   run the rest of the primary?
   > Model answer: Assume the second child is real until the primary proves otherwise.
   > Fresh crew if one's available — your search team just took a beating — restarting
   > from the found child's room outward: kids hide together, so under beds, closets, and
   > behind doors in the same and adjacent rooms first. The primary isn't complete until
   > every room is swept and called, and the parent's uncertainty never downgrades it.

## Stage: Floor Above and Wrap-Up

7. [common] (text) The fire is out in 2B. Who is in danger now, and where does search go
   next?
   > Model answer: The floor above — 3B took the smoke and heat through every penetration,
   > and its occupants may have sheltered or been overcome. The open center stairwell also
   > exposed every unit on this stair. Primary search of 3B, then the stairwell-adjacent
   > units, with the stairwell ventilated so it stops being a chimney.

8. [Engineer] (text) Command asks you to get a ladder to the 3B rear windows "in case."
   Where do you place the tip, and why does it matter?
   > Model answer: At or just below the sill, not into the opening — a sill-height tip
   > lets a firefighter enter and a victim come out headfirst onto the rungs, while a tip
   > in the window blocks the vent and the exit. Footed or tied, and announced: an
   > unannounced ladder at a window is one nobody uses when they need it.

9. [Officer] (text) Both children are out and viable; primaries are complete on 2 and 3.
   Before you report "primary all clear," what do you verify?
   > Model answer: That every searchable space was actually searched by a named crew —
   > all-clear is a reconstruction, not a feeling: which crew took which unit and rooms,
   > including the fire room after knockdown (fire rooms get skipped in the rush).
   > Then order secondaries by a different crew. "Primary all clear" transmitted on
   > assumption is how victims are found during overhaul.
