A fundamentals scenario for newer members: a nighttime house fire with the search
done by the numbers. Fire is in the kitchen at the rear of a two-story home at
01:20, attack line operating, and your two-person team is assigned primary search.
Whether anyone is home is unknown. Untracked: everyone answers everything.

## Stage: The Assignment

1. [common] (text) Define "primary search" in one or two sentences — and name the word
   in the definition that decides every tradeoff you'll make tonight.
   > Model answer: A rapid, systematic first search of all tenable areas for live
   > victims, done during active firefighting. The word is RAPID: primary trades
   > thoroughness for speed because survival is measured in minutes — the slow,
   > methodical version is the secondary, done after the fire is controlled, by a
   > different crew.

2. [common] (text) Kitchen fire at the rear, 1:20 AM, two-story house. Rank where you
   search first, second, third — and state the two principles that generate the ranking.
   > Model answer: First the area nearest the fire and its exhaust path (most danger,
   > least time), then the bedrooms — upstairs, at this hour — then the rest and the
   > exit paths. The two principles: search where victims are most imperiled first,
   > and search where victims are most PROBABLE — at 0120 that's beds, and the collision
   > of the two rules (bedrooms probable, fire floor perilous) is why the first-due
   > search often splits or the second crew takes the floor above.

3. [common] (text) Your partner asks whether you should follow the attack line in or take
   the stairs directly to the bedrooms. What does "search off the line" versus "search
   ahead of the line" actually trade?
   > Model answer: Searching off the line means protection — the hose is a guaranteed
   > exit path and water is nearby — at the cost of speed and coverage: you go where
   > the line goes. Searching away from it (stairs to the bedrooms) covers the
   > probable-victim area fast but leaves you navigating and self-protecting. With
   > fire controlled or knocking down, the bedrooms justify the direct route; with
   > fire still owning the first floor, freelancing past it is how searchers become
   > victims.

## Stage: Technique

4. [common] (text) Visibility upstairs is near zero. Describe the mechanics of searching
   the first bedroom: entry, pattern, what your hands and tools are doing, how you leave.
   > Model answer: Sound and enter low; pick a wall (left- or right-hand) and keep
   > contact — one hand or a tool on the wall, body sweeping the interior with the
   > other arm or a leg, tool extending your reach under and along furniture. Bed gets
   > special treatment: ON it and UNDER it, full length. Check behind the door and in
   > the closet. Leave by the same wall to the door, close the door if smoke is
   > pushing in, mark it searched per your system, and move to the next room. Same
   > hand, every room, no exceptions — the pattern is what finds the room's exit when
   > your air alarm goes off.

5. [common] (text) Why do instructors hammer "search the bed, then under it, then behind
   the door, then the closet"? What do those four locations share?
   > Model answer: They're where people are found: beds because that's where sleeping
   > victims stay (and children often don't wake to alarms), under beds and in closets
   > because frightened children HIDE from fire rather than flee, behind doors because
   > collapsing adults fall where they were reaching. The list encodes decades of
   > rescue and recovery locations — it's not technique trivia, it's a map of human
   > behavior in smoke.

6. [common] (text) Halfway through the second bedroom you hear glass break somewhere and
   the room warms noticeably. You have not finished the room. Walk through your
   decision, including who you talk to.
   > Model answer: Pause and assess against your exit: warming plus a new opening means
   > the fire may have found new air or extended. Radio your position and conditions —
   > "search, second floor, conditions warming, continuing" or "withdrawing" — and ask
   > command or attack for a status if it's not offered. Finish the room fast if
   > tenability holds; leave by your known wall if it doesn't, and SAY the room is
   > incomplete. An unfinished room reported is recoverable; an unfinished room
   > reported as searched is how victims are lost in "cleared" houses.

## Stage: Finding and Finishing

7. [common] (text) Under the window of the last bedroom your tool hits something soft:
   an adult, unresponsive. Give the sequence from this second until the victim is out of
   the house — including the radio traffic.
   > Model answer: Confirm it's a person, transmit immediately — "Search to Command:
   > victim found, second floor front bedroom, removing via interior stairs, send EMS
   > to side Alpha" — then move: grab-and-drag to the nearest safe exit route, partner
   > leading and clearing, victim dragged on their back protecting head and airway.
   > Radio again if the route changes. Don't start care in the hazard area; the
   > treatment for smoke inhalation is OUTSIDE. And the room you were in is now
   > unfinished — someone must be told.

8. [common] (text) Both floors are done and you report "primary all clear." Your officer
   asks the closing teaching question: what does "all clear" actually promise, and what
   does it NOT promise?
   > Model answer: It promises that every tenable area was rapidly searched by pattern
   > and no victims were found — a fast, good-faith sweep under fire conditions. It
   > does NOT promise the house is empty: zero visibility, closed voids, debris, and
   > speed all limit a primary, which is exactly why a secondary search — slow,
   > systematic, different crew, after control — is mandatory and not a formality.
   > Saying "primary all clear" out loud should feel like signing your name to
   > something; the secondary is the system admitting primaries can be wrong.
