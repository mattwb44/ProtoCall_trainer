House fire in the county at 16:45: a two-story farmhouse with fire showing from two
first-floor windows on the Bravo side. No hydrant system — you have your tank water,
a tender en route ten minutes behind you, and a pond across the road behind a fence
line. Occupants report everyone out.

## Stage: First Water

1. [common] (text) You have 1,000 gallons on the truck and a growing two-room fire.
   Frame the core problem of this fireground in one or two sentences.
   > Model answer: This is a water budget problem: every gallon must buy knockdown,
   > because resupply is ten minutes away and the fire compounds faster than tenders
   > arrive. The decision isn't just which line — it's whether the fire you can see is
   > small enough to kill with the water you actually have.

2. [Engineer] (text) Which line and nozzle do you put to work first, and roughly how long
   does your tank last flowing it?
   > Model answer: A 1¾" at 150–185 GPM gives you five to six minutes of flow from 1,000
   > gallons — enough to knock two rooms if the water hits the fire and not the smoke.
   > A 2½" would kill it faster but empties the tank in under four minutes with no
   > established supply; that's a defensible gamble only if you're confident in one fast,
   > decisive knock. Say which you chose and own the tradeoff.

3. [Firefighter] (text) On the nozzle at the Bravo-side door: two rooms going, moderate
   heat. How do you apply water so none of it is wasted?
   > Model answer: Straight stream off the ceiling of the fire room in controlled bursts
   > — steam conversion at the ceiling does the killing — then move and repeat, shutting
   > down between bursts to read the fire's response. Continuous fog into smoke looks
   > busy and empties a rural tank into the front lawn. Every open-nozzle second should
   > have a target.

4. [Officer] (text) Give your water supply orders for the incoming tender and the pond,
   before your tank runs dry.
   > Model answer: Tender nurses you directly on arrival (fastest gallons into the
   > fight), then establish a fill cycle: request additional tenders now — one tender is
   > a delay, not a supply — and assign the second-due engine to draft from the pond as a
   > fill site if access allows. The supply plan has to be running before the tank is
   > empty, because a dry attack engine resets the whole fireground.

## Stage: The Water Gap

5. [common] (text) Your tank hits quarter-full and the tender is still four minutes out.
   Fire is darkened in one room, still live in the second. What changes, and who decides?
   > Model answer: The engineer calls the water state early and loudly — quarter tank is
   > a command fact, not a pump-panel detail. The officer decides: press with short
   > bursts to hold the knock, or back crews out to the doorway and hold the fire in
   > check until supply lands. What's not acceptable is interior crews learning about the
   > water problem when the line goes soft.

6. [Engineer] (text) The tender arrives and wants to dump-and-go into a portable tank; you
   want to be nursed. Who's right, and what governs the choice?
   > Model answer: Depends on the operation's future: nursing is faster for THIS tank
   > refill, but a portable tank plus dump-and-go builds a shuttle that sustains flow
   > indefinitely with multiple tenders. One-tender fight nearing knockdown: nurse.
   > Extended operation or defensive turn: set the dump tank now, because you can't
   > build the shuttle later while starving the attack line.

7. [Firefighter] (text) During the low-water hold you notice fire has crept into the wall
   bay above the darkened room. You have maybe 100 gallons of margin. Do you open the wall?
   > Model answer: Yes — but with the officer's knowledge and a plan: a wall bay hit with
   > ten well-aimed gallons now is a room-and-attic fire that needs a thousand gallons in
   > fifteen minutes. Open low, hit it up the bay, confirm with the TIC. Deferring known
   > fire to save water is false economy; water spent stopping extension is the highest-
   > value water on a rural fireground.

## Stage: Sustained Supply

8. [Officer] (text) Knockdown is complete; overhaul in a farmhouse will take an hour.
   Design the water system for the rest of the incident.
   > Model answer: Portable tank at the engine, tender shuttle with at least two tenders
   > cycling to the pond fill site (or a hydrant in town if the pond fails), a designated
   > fill-site engine drafting, and a stated minimum reserve — e.g., never below half a
   > tank — protecting against rekindle. Release nothing until the shuttle has lapped
   > once and proven the cycle time.

9. [common] (text) After the incident, the crew debates whether the first line should have
   been the 2½". What facts from this fire decide that argument?
   > Model answer: The math: two rooms at roughly 300–400 square feet involved needed
   > about 150–200 GPM by standard fire-flow estimates, inside the 1¾"'s capability —
   > and the 1,000-gallon budget made flow duration as important as flow rate. The 2½"
   > wins when the volume of fire exceeds the small line's knockdown power; it loses when
   > it drains your only water before supply exists. The answer is arithmetic, not
   > preference.
