House fire at 14:30: a 1948 Cape Cod with smoke pushing from the gable vents and the
ridge. Occupants are out and confirmed. First line is being stretched; you have a
truck company and a second engine on scene.

## Stage: Size-Up

1. [common] (text) Smoke from the gable vents and ridge, nothing from the living-floor
   windows. Where is this fire, and what does a 1948 Cape tell you about fighting it?
   > Model answer: Attic fire. The good news about 1948: dimensional lumber — real 2x
   > rafters that hold under fire far longer than lightweight trusses, so a crew can work
   > under (and a vent crew on) this roof with a margin modern construction doesn't give.
   > The bad news about a Cape: finished attics mean knee walls with hidden void spaces
   > running the length of the house, and fire lives in them.

2. [Officer] (text) Attack needs to go through the interior stairs to the attic. What
   must ventilation NOT do until the line is ready, and what's your coordination call?
   > Model answer: Nobody opens the roof — or even the gable — until the line is at the
   > top of the stairs with water. Venting an underventilated attic fire early gives it
   > air and a direction, and the direction is at the stairs the crew is climbing. The
   > call: truck sets up and sounds the roof but cuts on my order, which comes when attack
   > reports "in position, charged line."

3. [Firefighter] (text) You're sent to open up ahead of the attack crew inside. Where does
   fire hide in a finished Cape attic, and what do you open first?
   > Model answer: The knee-wall voids — the triangular spaces behind the half-height
   > walls — plus the floor-to-eave bays feeding them. Open the knee-wall access panels
   > or cut the knee walls low ahead of the nozzle before flowing; hitting visible fire
   > while the voids reload is how attic fires "rekindle" twenty minutes later.

## Stage: Vent Execution

4. [common] (text) The attack crew is in position. Command orders vertical ventilation.
   Why vertical rather than horizontal here, and where exactly should the hole go?
   > Model answer: The fire and its heat are at the peak of the building — a hole in the
   > roof exhausts it straight up, lifting the attic environment for the crew instantly,
   > while gable windows vent only the ends and can drive fire across the attic laterally.
   > The hole goes as directly over the fire as the roof crew can safely work — exhaust at
   > the seat, not at the far end where it pulls fire through unburned attic.

5. [Engineer] (text) The truck is committed to the roof. A civilian tells you the "roof
   was redone" a few years ago. What does that mean for the roof crew and who needs to
   know?
   > Model answer: Possible second layer of roofing or new lightweight sheathing over the
   > old deck — heavier, slower to cut, and the added load plus concealed deterioration
   > changes the collapse math the crew based on "1948 dimensional lumber." The roof crew
   > and command need it immediately; sounding the roof just became non-negotiable and the
   > cut will take longer than planned.

6. [Firefighter] (text) You're on the roof. The hole is cut and pulled, and dark smoke
   rolls out but doesn't lift — it keeps boiling flat out of the hole. What is that
   telling you and what do you do?
   > Model answer: The fire isn't getting enough air to use the exhaust — it's still
   > underventilated and fuel-rich, and that boiling smoke can light the moment it finds
   > oxygen (including at your hole). Punch through the ceiling below the cut if you
   > haven't, report the conditions, and get off the roof on your planned exit route —
   > the vent is done; standing over a loaded attic adds nothing.

## Stage: Coordination Under Change

7. [common] (text) Moments after the roof opens, attack reports the attic "lit up brighter"
   before darkening down under their stream. Was the ventilation a failure? Explain what
   happened.
   > Model answer: No — that's exactly the sequence coordinated ventilation expects. The
   > new opening gave the oxygen-limited fire air and it grew toward the exhaust; because
   > the line was in position and flowing, the growth was met with water within seconds.
   > The same vent without a charged line in place turns "lit up brighter" into a crew
   > caught in the flow path. Timing, not the hole, is what made it work.

8. [Officer] (text) Attack reports knockdown but interior smoke conditions on the living
   floor are getting worse, not better. Diagnose it and give your next two orders.
   > Model answer: The attic is exhausting fine but the living floor has no flow — smoke
   > from the stairwell and pulled ceilings is banking with nowhere to go. Orders: set up
   > horizontal ventilation for floor 1 (open windows opposite the stair, or PPV at the
   > front door with the attic hole as exhaust once the fire is confirmed out), and have
   > the interior crew confirm the knee-wall voids are opened and checked before anyone
   > relaxes.

9. [Engineer] (text) Command wants PPV to clear the house. What must be true before the
   fan starts, and where does it go?
   > Model answer: Fire confirmed out — or at minimum fully controlled with lines in
   > place — and a clear exhaust opening sized larger than the intake, with no crews or
   > victims between intake and exhaust. Fan at the front door, angled so the cone seals
   > the frame, exhaust via the roof hole and floor-1 windows on the far side. PPV on a
   > live hidden fire (and Capes hide fire) turns a blower into a bellows.
